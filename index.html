Provable operator learning of size-transferable Trotter corrections

The finite-step defect of a product formula is the unique unitary residual $R_q=U\,S_q^\dagger$; we prove its generator $K_q=i\log R_q$ is geometrically local for the TFIM and learn it with one translation-equivariant network that transfers from 4–5-qubit training chains to 10-qubit chains under a closed-form $r\eta$ stability bound.

We recast product-formula (Trotter–Suzuki) error as an operator to be learned under provable guarantees. The residual factor is the unique norm-optimal left correction; Pauli-subspace projection of its Hermitian generator constitutes the unique Frobenius-optimal compression; and approximate residuals transform per-step error into global error linearly. For the open-boundary transverse-field Ising model (TFIM), the leading Strang generator carries Pauli weight $\le 3$, which reduces compilation to a local learning problem — one learnable from fixed-size patches without access to the dense $2^n$ propagator. Every value reported on this page is recomputed from deterministic dense-matrix simulation; quantities near $10^{-13}$ reflect the double-precision floor rather than physical error.

Background & Motivation

The finite-step defect, treated as a structured Lie-algebra operator.

Digital Hamiltonian simulation realizes $U(t)=\exp(-iHt)$ by composing readily exponentiated factors of a splitting $H=A+B$. Trotter–Suzuki product formulas remain the most transparent and hardware-aligned construction, and the contemporary theory — commutator-sensitive bounds together with locality and Lieb–Robinson arguments — sharpens the worst-case asymptotics. Every product-formula, multi-product, randomized, Magnus, qubitization, and Lie-algebraic compilation method nonetheless confronts the same object: the finite-step defect between the target and the implemented unitary. The present work isolates that defect, bounds it, compresses it, and ultimately learns it.

Hamiltonian simulation

For a two-term splitting $H=A+B$ with $A,B$ Hermitian, the order-$q$ step $S_q(\delta t)$ approximates $e^{-iH\delta t}$ to $O(\delta t^{q+1})$. The error accumulates over $r=t/\delta t$ steps; the benchmark adopts the spectral norm $\lVert U(t)-S_q(\delta t)^r\rVert_2$, which is basis-independent and corresponds operationally to a worst-case state-vector error.

Lie-algebra ideals & generator structure

Because $S_q$ is unitary, the residual $R_q=U S_q^\dagger$ is itself unitary and admits a Hermitian generator $K_q=i\log R_q$ within the dynamical Lie algebra. By the symmetric BCH expansion, the leading Strang defect is a linear combination of the double commutators $[A,[A,B]]$ and $[B,[A,B]]$ — nested-commutator structure that governs both the magnitude and the geometric locality of $K_q$.

Pauli-weight compressibility

Upon expansion of $K_q$ in the Pauli basis, the preponderance of its Frobenius mass concentrates on low-weight strings: for the $n=5$, $q=2$ TFIM generator at $\delta t=0.1$, weight $\le 3$ already accounts for $99.997\%$ of the squared-coefficient mass. The leading defect is local, whence a compact, implementable operator family suffices for the dominant correction.

The residual-learning gap this work addresses

The exact identity $R_q S_q = U$ is, in isolation, tautological — and the oracle that computes $R_q$ from the dense $e^{-iH\delta t}$ is as costly as exact simulation. The open question concerns the behavior of the residual under approximation, and whether a structured approximation may be obtained without the dense propagator. We resolve this gap by establishing stability and compression theorems and subsequently instantiating the residual generator as a learned, geometrically local, step-size-conditioned operator trained from oracle-free local patches, whose accuracy is bounded a priori rather than certified only retrospectively.

Contributions & Novelty

What is new, stated precisely.

The framework follows an oracle-to-algorithm progression: an ideal mathematical target (the exact residual generator), a collection of certificates that render its approximation provably safe, and a concrete learned approximation that eliminates the exponential bottleneck. The contributions, as stated in the manuscript, are the following.

  • (i) Exact factorization, uniqueness, optimality. We formalize residual-generator Trotter compilation (RGTC) and prove that $R_q=US_q^\dagger$ is the unique unitary left correction and a global minimizer of the one-step error in every unitarily invariant norm.
  • (ii) Multi-step stability theorems. Approximate residuals and generator approximations transform per-step error into global error linearly, with explicit $r\eta$ and $r\lVert K_q-\widehat K_q\rVert_2$ targets; the linear-in-$r$ rate is proven order-optimal ($o(r\eta)$ bounds fail to hold uniformly).
  • (iii) Frobenius-optimal compression. Pauli-subspace projection constitutes the unique Frobenius-optimal compressed residual generator, accompanied by a direct spectral-norm simulation certificate for projected generators.
  • (iv) TFIM locality theorem. The degree-three leading term of the Strang residual generator carries Pauli weight $\le 3$ — a concrete structural account of why $w\le 3$ projected residuals succeed for the TFIM.
  • (v) Learned residual mode (central practical advance). A single translation-equivariant network predicts the step-size-conditioned weight-$\le 3$ coefficients of $K_2$ from local couplings; it transfers from $n=4,5$ to $n\le 10$ ($R^2=0.9998$ on held-out sizes), reduces the uncorrected Strang error by $\sim\!38\text{--}46\times$, admits training without any dense $2^n$ oracle from fixed-size local patches, and surpasses the analytic leading-order BCH correction by more than an order of magnitude in the regime where the latter fails — all while satisfying the proven $r\eta$ bound.
  • (vi) Reproducibility. Deterministic dense-matrix experiments, figures, tables, and code recompute every reported value from first principles.

Methodological novelty beyond quantum simulation

The truncation error of a numerical integrator — conventionally an analytic object to be bounded — is shown to be a learnable operator that is geometrically local, conditioned on the discretization step, and transferable across system size, with approximation quality controlled a priori by a stability bound. This situates the work within structure-preserving, physics-constrained operator learning, in which a restricted symmetry-respecting hypothesis class renders generalization both data-efficient and certifiable.

Mathematics & Method

From the Hamiltonian to a certified learned correction.

The benchmark Hamiltonian is the open-boundary TFIM together with its canonical two-term splitting:

$$ H = A + B, \qquad A = J\sum_{j=1}^{n-1} Z_j Z_{j+1}, \qquad B = h\sum_{j=1}^{n} X_j. $$

Product-formula steps and the residual factor

The first-order Lie–Trotter step is $S_1(\delta t)=e^{-iB\delta t}e^{-iA\delta t}$ and the symmetric Strang step is $S_2(\delta t)=e^{-iB\delta t/2}e^{-iA\delta t}e^{-iB\delta t/2}$; higher even orders obey the Suzuki recursion $S_{2k}(\delta t)=S_{2k-2}(p_k\delta t)^2\,S_{2k-2}((1-4p_k)\delta t)\,S_{2k-2}(p_k\delta t)^2$ with $p_k=(4-4^{1/(2k-1)})^{-1}$ (elementary-factor counts $2,3,15,75,375$ for $q=1,2,4,6,8$). The residual factor is the unique left correction:

$$ R_q(\delta t) = U(\delta t)\,S_q(\delta t)^\dagger, \qquad R_q(\delta t)\,S_q(\delta t) = U(\delta t), \qquad \big[R_q(t/r)\,S_q(t/r)\big]^r = U(t). $$

$R_q$ is unitary and is the global minimizer of $\lVert U(\delta t)-L\,S_q(\delta t)\rVert$ over all left multipliers $L$, in every unitarily invariant norm; the minimum is zero and the minimizer is unique. This characterizes an oracle ceiling rather than a cost statement.

The residual generator and its norm scaling

Since $R_q$ is unitary, it possesses a Hermitian logarithm $K_q(\delta t)=i\log R_q(\delta t)$ (principal branch, well defined for small $\delta t$). If $S_q$ is of order $q$ with $\lVert U(\delta t)-S_q(\delta t)\rVert_2\le C_q|\delta t|^{q+1}$, then

$$ \lVert K_q(\delta t)\rVert_2 \;\le\; 2\,C_q\,|\delta t|^{q+1}, $$

so higher-order formulas yield smaller residual generators. For the Strang step ($q=2$), time-reversal symmetry annihilates the even-power term and the leading defect is of degree three:

$$ K_2(\delta t) = \delta t^3\, K_2^{(3)} + O(\delta t^5), \qquad K_2^{(3)} \in \operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R}\big\{\, i[A,[A,B]],\; i[B,[A,B]] \,\big\}. $$

Pauli-weight truncation, cumulative mass, and the locality theorem

With $\mathcal P_n=\{I,X,Y,Z\}^{\otimes n}$ and $\mathcal B_w=\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R}\{P:\operatorname{wt}(P)\le w\}$, the unique Frobenius-optimal compressed generator is the orthogonal Pauli projection

$$ \Pi_w K_q = \!\!\sum_{\operatorname{wt}(P)\le w}\!\! \frac{\operatorname{Tr}(P K_q)}{2^n}\,P, \qquad \lVert e^{-iK_q}-e^{-i\Pi_w K_q}\rVert_2 \le \lVert K_q-\Pi_w K_q\rVert_2 \le \lVert K_q-\Pi_w K_q\rVert_F. $$

For the open-boundary TFIM, $[Z_jZ_{j+1},X_j]=2iY_jZ_{j+1}$, and the union of two overlapping nearest-neighbor edges spans at most three sites; consequently every Pauli string in the leading double commutators carries weight $\le 3$, that is, $K_2^{(3)}\in\mathcal B_3$. Empirically (cumulative squared-coefficient mass $M(w)$ at $n=5$, $q=2$, $\delta t=0.1$): $M(1)=39.4\%$, $M(2)=66.1\%$, $M(3)=99.997\%$, with weights four and five capturing only subleading BCH structure near the double-precision floor.

The learned-residual model

Locality renders the map from couplings to generator coefficients itself local, so that a single per-site function applies at every site and at any chain length. On the disordered TFIM ($J_i,h_i\sim\mathcal U[0.5,1.5]$), each site is parameterized by the $T=48$ contiguous weight-$\le 3$ Pauli templates within its three-site window. A translation-equivariant MLP — 12 inputs (11 local couplings + step size $\delta t$) → two width-64 tanh layers → 48 coefficients, with weights shared across all sites and all chain lengths — predicts the $\delta t^{-3}$-scaled coefficients; the correction is reconstructed as $\delta t^3\widehat K_2^{(3)}$ and applied as $\widehat G(\delta t)=\exp(-i\delta t^3\widehat K_2^{(3)})\,S_2(\delta t)$. Sparse Pauli arithmetic evaluates generators up to $n=10$ without materializing dense Pauli matrices.

Coefficient error → global certificate

With $T$ templates per site and per-site coefficient error $\lVert c_j-\widehat c_j\rVert_2\le\delta_c$, the per-site operator error satisfies $\eta\le\sqrt T\,\delta_c$, and the size-transfer and stability theorems together yield, for $t=r\delta t$, $\lVert \widehat G(\delta t)^r - U(t)\rVert_2 \le r\,\delta t^3\, n\sqrt T\,\delta_c + r\,O(\delta t^5)$. The measured held-out accuracy ($\delta_c$ corresponding to relative $\ell_2$ error $1.32\times10^{-2}$, $R^2=0.9998$) thereby converts directly into an a priori global error bound, whose linear-in-$r$ growth the experiments confirm.

Experimental Design & Statistics

How the numbers are produced — and how they should be read.

Deterministic dense benchmarks

The fixed-time, projected, and order-scaling studies are single exact dense-matrix computations on a fixed grid ($J=h=1$, $t=1$, $r=10$). They involve zero stochastic sampling, and each reported value is therefore deterministic. The exact propagator $U(t)=e^{-iHt}$ is computed independently of the product-formula steps; spectral norms are obtained from singular values; and $K_q=i\log R_q$ is symmetrized to eliminate round-off skew-Hermitian contamination prior to projection.

Learned-transfer protocol

Disordered TFIM, $J_i,h_i\sim\mathcal U[0.5,1.5]$, with $\delta t$ sampled in $[0.05,0.30]$. Training: 220 realizations at each of $n=4,5$; 4000 full-batch Adam steps; a fixed seed (20240517) for the sampler, the split, and initialization. Test/transfer: held-out realizations at each size — 40 for $n=4\text{--}7$, 24 for $n=8$, 16 for $n=9$, and 12 for $n=10$. Reported errors are the mean $\pm$ one standard deviation over disordered chains; $R^2=0.9998$ denotes predicted-versus-exact coefficient parity on the held-out sizes $n=6,\dots,10$.

Two label sources

Oracle labels derive from the exact global residual generator (dense matrix logarithm) at the small training sizes. Oracle-free labels derive from the residual generator of a fixed-size local patch (radius two, $\le 7$ qubits), so that zero dense $2^n$ object enters training. At inference, either network observes only local couplings — never $U(\delta t)$. The two curves coincide, and the oracle-free model marginally improves upon the oracle-trained one at every size.

Finite-precision / float-floor caveat (oracle residuals)

The exact residual theorem is an algebraic identity; in floating-point arithmetic, every matrix exponential and multiplication contributes round-off. At low and moderate order the product-formula error dominates, and oracle RGTC reduces it by many orders of magnitude. For $q=8$ at $\delta t=0.1$, however, the baseline already approaches round-off, and the oracle construction introduces additional dense multiplications — whence its observed error may be of the same order as, or marginally larger than, the baseline. Values near $10^{-13}$–$10^{-14}$ reflect the double-precision floor; they are not physical error and do not contradict the factorization theorem. Compression results should be assessed at levels well above round-off (for instance, the $w=3,4$ projected rows).

Conclusion & Impact

An oracle target, made safe to approximate — and learned without the oracle.

The contribution resides in the combination: a uniquely defined oracle correction target; stability theorems translating residual error into global error; a generator perspective that exposes the defect as a compact Hermitian operator; a projection theorem rendering compressed generators well posed; a support theorem explaining why low-weight projection succeeds for the TFIM; and a demonstration that the residual generator can be learned as a geometrically local, size-transferable operator whose learned approximation inherits the stability guarantee.

Meaning

Compilation becomes a learning problem with an attached proof. A single per-site network, trained on small systems from oracle-free patches, corrects systems $32\times$ larger in Hilbert-space dimension, surpasses the analytic leading-order correction in the regime where it fails, and remains within a closed-form $r\eta$ error budget — converting a fidelity or learning-loss specification into a per-step synthesis target.

Transparent limitations

Oracle RGTC requires $U(\delta t)$ and is as costly as exact dense simulation. The oracle-free learned residual removes the dense $2^n$ oracle from training and conditions on $\delta t$, yet evaluation here remains dense (up to ten qubits), the local templates are TFIM-specific and leading-order, results employ double precision, and circuit-level synthesis of $\widehat K_q$ into native gates is not undertaken. These delineate the subsequent tasks; they do not invalidate the contribution.

Outlook

The most credible path forward employs the oracle residual as a target for algebraic, variational, and learned compilers, with rigorous certificates linking per-step residual error to global accuracy. Concrete continuations comprise: scaling evaluation beyond dense simulation; extending the local templates beyond the TFIM and to higher orders; replacing patch labels with classical-shadow or commutator estimates on hardware; and synthesizing the learned generator $e^{-i\widehat K_q}$ into native gates, with the stability and Duhamel bounds serving as the operator-norm target. The dense-linear-algebra, parameter-sweep workload maps naturally onto GPU acceleration for larger offline training sets. These constitute implementation rather than a change of principle.
