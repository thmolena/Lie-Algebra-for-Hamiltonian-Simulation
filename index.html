Provable operator learning of size-transferable Trotter corrections

The finite-step defect of a product formula is the unique unitary residual $R_q=U\,S_q^\dagger$. Its generator $K_q=i\log R_q$ is provably geometrically local for the TFIM, and one translation-equivariant network learns it well enough to transfer from 4–5-qubit training chains to 10-qubit chains under a closed-form $r\eta$ stability bound — turning circuit compilation into a certified learning problem.

Molena Huynh
North Carolina State University · molena.huynh@jmp.com

Digital Hamiltonian simulation by Trotter–Suzuki product formulas incurs a finite-step error fixed by nested commutator defects, which prior theory bounds but does not constructively remove. This work recasts that error as an operator to be learned under provable guarantees. The residual factor of a product-formula step is the unique unitary left correction to the exact propagator; we prove it is optimal in every unitarily invariant norm, that projecting its Hermitian generator onto a fixed operator subspace is the unique Frobenius-optimal compression, and that an approximate residual converts per-step error into global error linearly. For the open-boundary transverse-field Ising model (TFIM) the leading Strang generator carries Pauli weight at most three, collapsing compilation to a local learning problem. Every value on this page is recomputed from deterministic dense-matrix simulation; quantities near $10^{-13}$ reflect the double-precision floor rather than physical error.

Background and Motivation

Product-formula simulation, and the open problem of a cheap, certified correction.

Digital Hamiltonian simulation realizes $U(t)=\exp(-iHt)$ by composing the readily exponentiated factors of a splitting $H=A+B$. Since Lloyd's observation that local quantum systems are efficiently simulable, Trotter–Suzuki product formulas have remained the most transparent and hardware-aligned construction, with higher even orders generated by the Suzuki recursion. Contemporary theory — commutator-sensitive bounds combined with locality and Lieb–Robinson arguments — sharpens the worst-case asymptotics considerably. Yet product formulas, multi-product formulas, truncated Taylor methods, quantum signal processing and qubitization, randomized formulas, and Magnus-expansion algorithms all confront the same object: the finite-step defect between the target propagator and the implemented unitary. That defect, not its bound, is what this work corrects.

The product-formula step

For a two-term splitting $H=A+B$ with $A,B$ Hermitian, the order-$q$ step $S_q(\delta t)$ approximates $e^{-iH\delta t}$ to $O(\delta t^{q+1})$, and the error accumulates over $r=t/\delta t$ steps. We measure it in the spectral norm $\lVert U(t)-S_q(\delta t)^r\rVert_2$: basis-independent, and operationally the worst-case state-vector error.

The defect as a commutator object

The leading defect is fixed entirely by nested commutators. By the symmetric Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff expansion, the Strang defect carries no even-power term; its first nonzero contribution is a linear combination of the double commutators $[A,[A,B]]$ and $[B,[A,B]]$. This commutator structure dictates both the magnitude of the correction and its geometric locality — the property the rest of this work exploits.

The open problem

Existing analysis bounds the defect but does not deliver a cheap, certified correction carrying an a priori guarantee. Four obstacles stand in the way: exposing the defect as an implementable operator, compressing it without discarding the dominant correction, learning it without ever forming the dense $2^n$ propagator, and certifying it a priori rather than only a posteriori. This work resolves all four.

Two operating modes, deliberately separated

Oracle RGTC computes the residual from the exact dense matrix exponential. It is neither scalable nor claimed to be a deployable quantum algorithm; its role is to fix the target — the best possible one-step left correction. Compressed RGTC replaces the oracle with a Hermitian generator drawn from a restricted family, instantiated here by Pauli-weight projection and, centrally, by operator learning. The distinction is the substance of the contribution: the identity $U S_q^\dagger S_q = U$ alone is tautological, so a useful result must quantify exactly what is lost when the exact residual is approximated.

Mathematical Preliminaries

The transverse-field Ising model, the product-formula step, and the residual generator.

Fix a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}\cong\mathbb{C}^d$ with $d=2^n$ and a Hermitian $H=H^\dagger$ whose exact time-$t$ propagator is $U(t)=e^{-iHt}$. A two-term splitting $H=A+B$, with $A,B$ Hermitian and each easier to exponentiate than their sum, covers the standard even–odd and diagonal–transverse splittings of spin models. The running benchmark is the open-boundary TFIM.

Hamiltonian and splitting

$$ H = A + B,\qquad A = J\sum_{j=1}^{n-1} Z_j Z_{j+1},\qquad B = h\sum_{j=1}^{n} X_j. $$

A canonical many-body benchmark: it admits a clean two-term splitting, is exactly densely simulable at $n\le 6$, and retains strongly noncommuting local structure. The disordered variant draws site-dependent couplings $J_i,h_i\sim\mathcal{U}[0.5,1.5]$.

Product-formula steps

$$ S_1(\delta t)=e^{-iB\delta t}e^{-iA\delta t},\qquad S_2(\delta t)=e^{-iB\delta t/2}\,e^{-iA\delta t}\,e^{-iB\delta t/2}, $$
$$ S_{2k}(\delta t)=S_{2k-2}(p_k\delta t)^2\,S_{2k-2}\!\big((1-4p_k)\delta t\big)\,S_{2k-2}(p_k\delta t)^2,\qquad p_k=\big(4-4^{1/(2k-1)}\big)^{-1}. $$

These are the first-order Lie–Trotter step, the symmetric second-order Strang step, and the Suzuki recursion that generates the higher even orders. Their elementary factor counts are $2,3,15,75,375$ for $q=1,2,4,6,8$, and every $S_q$ is unitary for real $\delta t$.

Residual factor and residual generator

$$ R_q(\delta t)=U(\delta t)\,S_q(\delta t)^\dagger,\qquad G_q(\delta t)=R_q(\delta t)\,S_q(\delta t),\qquad R_q(\delta t)=e^{-iK_q(\delta t)},\quad K_q(\delta t)=i\log R_q(\delta t). $$

The residual factor $R_q$ defines a corrected step $G_q=R_qS_q$ that equals $U(\delta t)$ exactly in exact arithmetic. Because $R_q$ is unitary it carries a Hermitian residual generator $K_q$, well defined as a principal logarithm once $\delta t$ is small enough that $R_q$ lies near the identity (eigenphases taken in $[-\pi,\pi]$). This generator is the object the learning problem targets. All reported norms are spectral norms $\lVert\cdot\rVert_2$ unless stated otherwise; the Frobenius norm $\lVert\cdot\rVert_F$ governs Pauli-coefficient compression.

Pauli expansion and weight projection

$$ \Pi_w K_q=\sum_{\operatorname{wt}(P)\le w}\frac{\operatorname{Tr}(P K_q)}{2^n}\,P,\qquad \mathcal{B}_w=\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb{R}}\{P\in\{I,X,Y,Z\}^{\otimes n}:\operatorname{wt}(P)\le w\}. $$

The normalized Pauli strings $P/\sqrt{2^n}$ form an orthonormal basis under the Hilbert–Schmidt inner product, so $\Pi_w$ is exactly the orthogonal projection onto the weight-$\le w$ Pauli subspace $\mathcal{B}_w$ — the algebraic fact that makes weight truncation canonical rather than ad hoc.

Problem Formulation

Predicting the local weight-$\le 3$ generator coefficients without ever forming the $2^n$ oracle.

The exact residual generator $K_q$ is defined through the dense $2^n$ propagator, making it as costly as exact simulation — useless as a target unless it can be predicted indirectly. The objective is therefore an implementable generator $\widehat{K}_q$ that reproduces the dominant correction while never accessing $U(\delta t)$ at evaluation scale. The locality theorem makes this well posed: because the leading Strang generator is supported on geometrically local Pauli strings of weight at most three, the map from local couplings to residual coefficients is itself local, and a fixed-radius predictor suffices.

Local parameterization

At each site $j$, the per-site generator $h_j$ expands in the $T=48$ weight-$\le 3$ Pauli templates supported within the three-site window $\{j-1,j,j+1\}$, $h_j=\sum_{\alpha=1}^{T} c_{j,\alpha}P_{j,\alpha}$, with $K_2^{(3)}=\sum_j h_j$. The only unknowns are the coefficients $c_{j,\alpha}$.

Translation-equivariant predictor

A single per-site network maps the window's local couplings (11 of them) plus the step size $\delta t$ — 12 inputs — through two width-64 $\tanh$ layers to the 48 $\delta t^{-3}$-scaled coefficients, with weights shared across every site and every chain length. This weight sharing is precisely the inductive bias that lets a model trained on small chains correct larger ones.

Oracle-free objective

Training labels come from the residual generator of a fixed-size local patch (radius two, at most seven qubits), so no dense $2^n$ object ever enters training, and at inference the network sees only local couplings, never $U(\delta t)$. The correction is reassembled as $\delta t^3$ times the network output and applied as $\exp(-i\delta t^3\widehat{K}_2^{(3)})\,S_2$.

Principal Contributions

Six claims, each self-contained and provable.

  1. An exact, optimal residual target. The residual $R_q=US_q^\dagger$ is the unique unitary left correction satisfying $R_qS_q=U$, and it globally minimizes $\lvert\lvert\lvert U-L S_q\rvert\rvert\rvert$ over all left multipliers in every unitarily invariant norm, attaining the minimum value zero.
  2. Stability under approximation. With $\eta=\lVert\widehat{R}_q-R_q\rVert_2$, the global error obeys $\lVert\widehat{G}_q^{\,r}-U(t)\rVert_2\le r\eta$ for unitary $\widehat{R}_q$ (and $\le r\eta\,e^{(r-1)\eta}$ in general); a matching lower bound proves the linear-in-$r$ rate is order-optimal.
  3. Canonical, certified compression. Orthogonal projection $\Pi_{\mathcal B}K$ onto any real Pauli subspace is the unique minimizer of $\lVert K-L\rVert_F$, and it carries a direct spectral-norm certificate $\lVert e^{-iK}-e^{-i\Pi_{\mathcal B}K}\rVert_2\le\lVert K-\Pi_{\mathcal B}K\rVert_F$.
  4. Provable locality for the TFIM. For the open-boundary TFIM the degree-three homogeneous term of $K_2$ is a real combination of Pauli strings of weight at most three: $K_2(\delta t)=\delta t^3 K_2^{(3)}+O(\delta t^5)$ with $K_2^{(3)}\in\mathcal{B}_3$ — the structural fact that makes per-site learning possible.
  5. A learned, size-transferable generator. A single translation-equivariant network predicts the step-conditioned local weight-$\le 3$ coefficients of $K_2$ from local couplings alone, trains without any dense $2^n$ oracle, transfers from $n=4,5$ to $n=10$ ($R^2=0.9998$ on held-out sizes), cuts uncorrected Strang error by $38\text{--}46\times$, and surpasses the analytic leading-order correction by more than an order of magnitude where the latter fails — all while honoring the proven $r\eta$ bound.
  6. Deterministic, falsifiable evidence. Dense-matrix experiments, figures, tables, and code recompute every reported number from first principles, with unitarity and same-order consistency checks that would expose any fabrication or implementation error.
Methodological Novelty

A learned, geometrically local, step-conditioned, size-transferable error operator.

Prior product-formula analysis treats truncation error as an analytic object to be bounded. The shift here is to treat it as a learnable operator that is simultaneously local, step-conditioned, and size-transferable — four properties no prior bound supplies jointly — and whose approximation quality is controlled a priori by a proven stability bound rather than validated only a posteriori.

Prior product-formula bounds

  • Estimate the defect magnitude (worst-case or commutator-scaling), but supply no constructive correction.
  • Fix the error analytically from the formula, with no learned or data-driven component.
  • The leading-order BCH correction captures only the $\delta t^3$ term and degrades rapidly as the step grows.
  • Establish accuracy only a posteriori, by evaluating the implemented unitary.

The learned residual operator (this work)

  • Learned: one translation-equivariant network maps local couplings to generator coefficients, never touching the propagator.
  • Geometrically local: proven weight-$\le 3$ support collapses compilation to a per-site problem over a fixed template family.
  • Step-conditioned: $\delta t$ enters as an input, so the correction tracks the working-$\delta t$ coefficients precisely where the $\delta t^3$-only baseline fails.
  • Size-transferable: weight sharing keeps the per-site error $\eta$ independent of $n$, so one network corrects far larger chains under an a priori $r\eta$ certificate.

Methodological interest beyond quantum simulation

The truncation error of a numerical integrator — conventionally an analytic object to be bounded — is shown here to be a learnable operator: geometrically local, conditioned on the discretization step, transferable across system size, and certified a priori by a proven stability bound. This situates the work within structure-preserving, physics-constrained operator learning, where a restricted, symmetry-respecting hypothesis class is exactly what renders generalization both data-efficient and certifiable.

Theoretical Guarantees

The formal results, transcribed verbatim with one-line interpretations.

All norms are spectral norms unless stated otherwise. Each statement below is transcribed exactly from the manuscript's Methods and paired with its operational reading.

Theorem 1 · Exact residual factorization and uniqueness

$R_q=US_q^\dagger$ is unitary and the unique $L$ with $LS_q=U$; hence $R_qS_q=U$ and $[R_q(t/r)S_q(t/r)]^r=U(t)$.

Reading: for time-independent $H$ the residual is an exact, uniquely defined correction target — not an approximation.

Theorem 2 · Optimality among all left corrections

For every unitarily invariant norm, $R_q$ globally minimizes $\mathcal{J}(L)=\lvert\lvert\lvert U-LS_q\rvert\rvert\rvert$; the minimum is zero and the minimizer is unique.

Reading: no left multiplier can beat $R_q$ in any such norm, since its error is already zero. This bounds optimality, not cost.

Theorem 3 · Small-step residual-generator bound

If $\lVert U-S_q\rVert_2\le C_q\lvert\delta t\rvert^{q+1}\le 1$, then $K_q$ exists with phases in $[-\pi,\pi]$ and $\lVert K_q(\delta t)\rVert_2\le 2C_q\lvert\delta t\rvert^{q+1}$.

Reading: the generator is small at small steps and shrinks with order $q$, leaving a low-weight or learned approximation a small target to capture.

Theorem 4 · Multi-step stability for approximate residuals

With $\eta=\lVert\widehat{R}_q-R_q\rVert_2$, $\;\lVert\widehat{G}_q^{\,r}-U(t)\rVert_2\le \eta\sum_{j=0}^{r-1}(1+\eta)^j\le r\eta\,e^{(r-1)\eta}$; if $\widehat{R}_q$ is unitary, the sharp bound $\le r\eta$ holds.

Reading: per-step residual error accumulates only linearly, so a global fidelity target $\epsilon$ translates directly into a per-step synthesis target $\eta\le\epsilon/r$.

Theorem 5 · Tightness of the stability bound

For every $\eta\in(0,\pi]$ there is a unitary $\widehat{R}_q$ with $\lVert\widehat{R}_q-R_q\rVert_2\le\eta$ and $\lVert\widehat{G}_q^{\,r}-U(r\delta t)\rVert_2\ge\tfrac{2}{\pi}\,r\eta$ whenever $r\eta\le\pi$.

Reading: the linear-in-$r$ rate cannot be improved — no bound of the form $o(r\eta)$ holds uniformly in $r$.

Theorem 6 · Duhamel bound for generator approximation

For Hermitian $K,\widehat{K}$, $\;\lVert e^{-iK}-e^{-i\widehat{K}}\rVert_2\le\lVert K-\widehat{K}\rVert_2$; hence $\lVert\widehat{G}_q^{\,r}-U(t)\rVert_2\le r\lVert K_q-\widehat{K}_q\rVert_2$.

Reading: generator error passes directly to unitary error, so controlling $K_q$ in operator norm is enough to control the global simulation error.

Theorem 7 · Frobenius-optimal Pauli compression

$\Pi_{\mathcal B}K$ uniquely minimizes $\lVert K-L\rVert_F$ over a Pauli subspace $\mathcal{B}$, and $\lVert e^{-iK}-e^{-i\Pi_{\mathcal B}K}\rVert_2\le\lVert K-\Pi_{\mathcal B}K\rVert_2\le\lVert K-\Pi_{\mathcal B}K\rVert_F$.

Reading: Pauli-weight truncation is the canonical compression, and the Frobenius norm certifies exactly how much it costs.

Theorem 8 · Leading Strang residual support for the open-boundary TFIM

$K_2(\delta t)=\delta t^3 K_2^{(3)}+O(\delta t^5)$ with $K_2^{(3)}\in\mathcal{B}_3$: the degree-three term is a real combination of Pauli strings of weight $\le 3$.

Reading: the dominant defect lives entirely on three-site windows — the structural reason weight-$3$ projection and per-site learning succeed for the TFIM. (Leading-order and model-specific; higher BCH terms can enlarge the support.)

Theorem 9 · Size-transfer error bound for learned local residual generators

If a translation-invariant per-site predictor has $\lVert\widehat{h}_j-h_j\rVert_2\le\eta$ ($\eta$ independent of $n$), then $\lVert\widehat{K}_2^{(3)}-K_2^{(3)}\rVert_2\le n\eta$ and, for $t=r\delta t$, $\lVert\widehat{G}^r-U(t)\rVert_2\le r\,\delta t^3 n\eta + r\,O(\delta t^5)$.

Reading: weight sharing makes the per-site error size-independent, so a model trained on small chains carries a provable global certificate to much larger ones.

Corollary · Projected residual simulation certificate

For $\widehat{G}_{q,w}=e^{-i\Pi_w K_q}S_q$, $\;\lVert\widehat{G}_{q,w}^{\,r}-U(t)\rVert_2\le r\lVert K_q-\Pi_w K_q\rVert_2$; target accuracy $\epsilon$ holds whenever $\lVert K_q-\Pi_w K_q\rVert_2\le\epsilon/r$.

Reading: the per-step coefficient error of a Theorem-9 predictor (via $\eta\le\sqrt{T}\,\delta_c$, $T=48$) maps directly onto an a priori global error budget.

Significance and Impact

An oracle target, made safe to approximate — then learned without the oracle.

The contribution is the chain of pieces fitting together: a uniquely defined oracle correction target; stability theorems that turn residual error into global error; a generator perspective that exposes the defect as a compact Hermitian operator; a projection theorem that makes compressed generators well posed; a support theorem that explains why low-weight projection succeeds for the TFIM; and, finally, a demonstration that the residual generator can be learned as a geometrically local, size-transferable operator whose approximation inherits the stability guarantee. No single piece is the result; their composition is.

Consequence for quantum simulation

Compilation becomes a learning problem that ships with its own proof. A single per-site network, trained on small systems from oracle-free patches, corrects a system $32\times$ larger in Hilbert-space dimension, beats the analytic leading-order correction precisely in the regime where it fails, and stays inside a closed-form $r\eta$ budget — turning a fidelity or learning-loss specification directly into a per-step synthesis target. This is an "oracle-to-algorithm" pattern: an ideal mathematical object, the residual generator, around which efficient block encodings, compilers, or learned estimators can be built. Deployed as a correction layer after a product-formula block, it composes with multi-product, randomized, qubitization, and Cartan-decomposition strategies rather than competing with them.

Scope and limitations

Oracle RGTC requires $U(\delta t)$ and is as costly as exact dense simulation. The oracle-free learned residual removes the dense $2^n$ oracle from training and conditions on $\delta t$, while evaluation here remains dense (up to ten qubits); the local templates are TFIM-specific and leading-order; all results use double precision and small systems; and circuit-level synthesis of $\widehat{K}_q$ into native gates is left to future work. Other Hamiltonians may require different generator families and retraining. The paper establishes correction and compression guarantees rather than a universal asymptotic quantum speedup. These define the next steps and stand alongside the present contribution.

Outlook

The most credible path forward treats the oracle residual as a target for algebraic, variational, and learned compilers, with rigorous certificates linking per-step residual error to global accuracy. Concrete continuations follow directly: scaling evaluation beyond dense simulation; extending the local templates past the TFIM and to higher orders; replacing patch labels with classical-shadow or commutator estimates on hardware; and synthesizing the learned generator $e^{-i\widehat{K}_q}$ into native gates, with the stability and Duhamel bounds supplying the operator-norm target. The dense-linear-algebra, parameter-sweep workload maps naturally onto GPU acceleration for larger offline training sets. Each is implementation, not a change of principle.

Reproducibility

A pip-installable artifact that recomputes every number from first principles.

All data are generated by deterministic scripts directly from the Hamiltonian definition; every result is computed, never transcribed by hand. The accompanying artifact is the pip-installable package lieideal-hs 1.0.0 (MIT, requires Python $\ge 3.10$) at submission/code. Built-in checks verify the unitarity of $S_q$, $R_q$, and the corrected steps; compare same-order $S_q$ against $R_qS_q$; recompute $U(t)$ exactly and densely; persist raw CSV/JSON before any plotting; and fix seeds for the learned-residual sampler, the data split, and network initialization — the safeguards that make fabrication or silent error detectable.
