Lie GPT for Hamiltonian Simulation:
Generator Prediction in Dynamical Lie Algebras
Beyond Product Formulas

Every standard Hamiltonian simulation method fixes its generator set to at most $\{A,B\}$ before constructing any circuit. The Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff expansion reveals what is lost: the commutator $[A,B]$ accumulates as systematic, avoidable error. Lie GPT removes this restriction by treating simulation as generator prediction within the dynamical Lie algebra — a principled DLA in which each new generator cancels one additional BCH error order. The central result is a 10× improvement theorem over Trotter–Suzuki with no variational optimization. The same dynamical Lie algebra (DLA) extends naturally to a learned, GRU-based Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff (BCH) approach that guarantees exact unitarity and provable long-time stability by construction.

The Lie GPT Framework

Product formulas approximate $e^{-it(A+B)}$ by products of $e^{-i\delta t A}$ and $e^{-i\delta t B}$. The BCH expansion shows exactly what this costs: $$e^{-i\delta t B}e^{-i\delta t A} = \exp\!\Bigl[-i\delta t(A+B)+\tfrac{\delta t^2}{2}[A,B]+O(\delta t^3)\Bigr],$$ so $[A,B]$ — a member of the dynamical Lie algebra $\mathcal{L}(A,B)$ — enters unavoidably as the leading error term. Every Trotter or Suzuki formula excludes at least some DLA generators; Lie GPT includes them explicitly.

The Dynamical Lie Algebra (DLA) and the GPT Hierarchy

Given $H = A + B$, the dynamical Lie algebra is $\mathcal{L}(A,B) = \mathrm{span}\{A,\,B,\,[A,B],\,[A,[A,B]],\,[B,[A,B]],\,\ldots\}.$ Lie GPT-$k$ selects the depth-$k$ DLA truncation as its circuit generator set:

GPT-0 — first-order Trotter

Generators: $\{A,\,B\}$

Local error $O(\delta t^2)$. The commutator $[A,B]$ is absent and accumulates as the leading BCH residual. GPT-0 is exactly Trotter-1 — the baseline against which all improvements are measured.

GPT-1 — one commutator added

Generators: $\{A,\,B,\,[A,B]\}$

The propagator $V_1(\delta t) = e^{-\delta t^2[A,B]/2}\,e^{-i\delta t B}\,e^{-i\delta t A}$ cancels the $O(\delta t^2)$ BCH error exactly, reducing local error to $O(\delta t^3)$. This matches Suzuki-2 in error order using one extra layer, not three exponentials.

GPT-2 — two depth-2 generators

Generators: $\{A,\,B,\,[A,B],\,C_A,\,C_B\}$

$C_A=[A,[A,B]]$, $C_B=[B,[A,B]]$ cancel the $O(\delta t^3)$ residual. Corrected coefficients $\alpha_2=-\tfrac{1}{6}$, $\alpha_3=-\tfrac{1}{3}$ (naïve power-counting gives the wrong sign; this error is identified and corrected in the paper) yield local error $O(\delta t^4)$, matching Suzuki-4 asymptotic scaling.

The GPT Analogy

The name “Lie GPT” reflects a structural parallel with language models. A GPT model defines a dynamical Lie algebra (DLA) of tokens and a Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff (BCH) approach for selecting the next token. Here the DLA provides the generator set (generators are tokens) and the BCH expansion provides the selection rule: at each level $k$, include the generator that maximally cancels the next error term. When the BCH approach is analytically fixed, the result is the 10× improvement theorem (paper). When the BCH approach is learned from quantum trajectory data via a GRU constrained to the DLA, the result is the stable, data-efficient framework of the notebooks.

The 10× Improvement Theorem

The central theoretical result of the paper. For any order $k$, an explicit threshold $\delta t^{(k)}$ determined by BCH prefactors of $H$ exists such that Lie GPT-$k$ achieves spectral error at least one order of magnitude smaller than the $k$-th order Trotter–Suzuki formula for all $\delta t\leq\delta t^{(k)}$.

Theorem (10× Improvement)

For all $\delta t \leq \delta t^{(k)}$:

$\|e^{-i\delta t H} - U_k(\delta t)\|_2 \;\leq\; \tfrac{1}{10}\,\|e^{-i\delta t H} - U^{(k)}_{\mathrm{TS}}(\delta t)\|_2$

where $U_k$ is the Lie GPT-$k$ propagator and $U^{(k)}_{\mathrm{TS}}$ is the $k$-th order Trotter–Suzuki formula. For $k=1$: $\delta t^{(1)} = \|[A,B]\|_2 \big/ \bigl(10\bigl(\tfrac{1}{3}\|[A,[A,B]]\|_2 + \tfrac{2}{3}\|[B,[A,B]]\|_2\bigr)\bigr).$

Proof mechanism. GPT-1 prepends $e^{-\delta t^2[A,B]/2}$ to the Trotter step. By the BCH product formula (Proposition: BCH cancellation), this cancels the $O(\delta t^2)$ Trotter error exactly, leaving a residual of $O(\delta t^3)$. The ratio of the $O(\delta t^2)$ Trotter error to the $O(\delta t^3)$ GPT-1 error grows as $\delta t^{-1}$ and exceeds 10 below the threshold. The improvement propagates to $N$-step global error via a standard telescoping argument: the global ratio is $\approx (C_{\mathrm{Trot}}/C_{\mathrm{GPT1}})\cdot(N/t)$, growing with $N$ at fixed $T$. GPT-2 cancels the $O(\delta t^3)$ residual with corrected coefficients $\alpha_2=-\tfrac{1}{6}$, $\alpha_3=-\tfrac{1}{3}$; the naïve sign $+1/12$ reinforces rather than cancels the error.

Three Notebooks: Extending Lie GPT to Learned Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff (BCH) Approaches

The paper establishes the analytical half of the Lie GPT framework: BCH-optimal generator selection yields 10× improvement with analytically fixed coefficients. The natural generalization asks: what if the Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff (BCH) selection is learned from quantum trajectory data rather than analytically derived? The three notebooks answer this by implementing Lie GPT as a GRU augmented with a zero-parameter Lie Constraint Layer that forces all output generators to lie in $\mathfrak{su}(2)$, making unitarity structurally impossible to violate. The three notebooks form one continuous arc: foundations (why and how the constraint works) → long-time stability (the primary payoff of exact unitarity) → data efficiency and noise robustness (why less data suffices and the guarantee is noise-proof). Each notebook is available as a pre-rendered HTML page and as an executable .ipynb source.

Notebook 1 — Foundations
Architecture & Unitarity Guarantee
Establishes the mathematical foundation shared by both the analytical and learned frameworks. Visualizes the $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ basis ($\sigma_x,\sigma_y,\sigma_z$) and the Levi-Civita structure constants $[X_i,X_j]=\sum_k c_{ij}^k X_k$, which encode the commutator structure of the DLA. Implements the Lie Constraint Layer: the GRU outputs three real scalars $\theta_i$; the layer assembles $H=\sum_i\theta_i\sigma_i$, which is Hermitian by construction, so $U = e^{-iH\Delta t}$ is always unitary. Quantified over $10^5$ random Hamiltonians: Lie GPT unitarity violation $\|U^\dagger U-I\|_F\approx10^{-16}$; a soft-penalty GRU reaches $\sim10^{-2}$; an unconstrained GRU reaches $\sim10^{-1}$. Bloch sphere trajectories confirm exact norm preservation on $S^2$.

Notebook 2 — Primary Result
Long-Time Stability
Having established that the Lie Constraint Layer guarantees unitarity at every individual step, this notebook tests the compounding consequence: exact per-step unitarity prevents error from accumulating super-linearly during extended rollouts. All four models (Lie GPT, GRU, GRU+unitarity penalty, MLP) are trained on sequences of length $T=25$ and evaluated at $T=200$ — an $8\times$ extrapolation beyond the training horizon without retraining. Lie GPT state error stays bounded; the log-log slope confirms the $O(T)$ linear bound of Theorem 1. Every baseline diverges — including the GRU with a soft unitarity penalty, establishing that the hard architectural constraint is not merely convenient but strictly necessary.

Notebook 3 — Supporting Experiments
Data Efficiency & Noise Robustness
With long-time stability demonstrated, the final notebook asks: does the Lie Constraint Layer also reduce the training data requirement? Yes — and Theorem 2 explains why: constraining the output to the $k=3$-dimensional $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra reduces Rademacher complexity by $\sqrt{k/n^2}$ relative to the unconstrained matrix class. The GRU no longer needs to learn unitarity from data; its full capacity is available for learning dynamics. Verified over a sweep of training set sizes $N\in\{50,100,250,500,1000,2000,5000\}$: Lie GPT reaches a given accuracy threshold with $\sim3\times$ fewer trajectories. The noise robustness experiment completes the case: at all input noise levels $\sigma\in\{0,0.05,0.1,0.2,0.5\}$, the hard constraint keeps unitarity violation at $\varepsilon_\text{machine}$ regardless of input quality.
