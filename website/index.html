Trotter error is an operator: provably optimal, geometrically local, and learnable across system size.

Molena Huynh · North Carolina State University · molena.huynh@jmp.com

The finite-step defect of a product formula is exactly the unique unitary residual $R_q=U\,S_q^\dagger$. Its generator $K_q=i\log R_q$ is provably geometrically local for the TFIM, and a single translation-equivariant network learns it from local couplings alone, transferring from 4–5-qubit chains to 10-qubit chains under a closed-form $r\eta$ error budget.

This work reframes product-formula (Trotter–Suzuki) error not as a quantity to bound but as an operator to learn under exact guarantees. Three structural facts make this tractable: the residual factor is the unique norm-optimal left correction, projection onto low-weight Pauli operators is the unique Frobenius-optimal compression of its generator, and approximate residuals convert per-step error into global error linearly. Every value below is recomputed from deterministic dense-matrix simulation. This page is a condensed brief; the full project report gives the complete theory, statistics, and an interactive demo.

Background & Method

The finite-step defect is a structured operator in the dynamical Lie algebra.

Digital Hamiltonian simulation realizes $U(t)=\exp(-iHt)$ by composing the factors of a splitting $H=A+B$. The order-$q$ step $S_q(\delta t)$ never reproduces $U$ exactly, and the entire defect is captured by a single object: the residual $R_q=US_q^\dagger$, the unique unitary left correction. Its Hermitian generator $K_q=i\log R_q$ lives in the dynamical Lie algebra, and its commutator structure is what makes it both local and learnable. The benchmark Hamiltonian is the open-boundary TFIM:

$$ H = A + B, \qquad A = J\sum_{j=1}^{n-1} Z_j Z_{j+1}, \qquad B = h\sum_{j=1}^{n} X_j. $$

Generator structure & locality

For Strang ($q=2$), $K_2(\delta t)=\delta t^3 K_2^{(3)}+O(\delta t^5)$, and the leading term $K_2^{(3)}$ is built entirely from the double commutators $[A,[A,B]]$ and $[B,[A,B]]$. A locality theorem shows that for the open-boundary TFIM these commutators carry Pauli weight $\le 3$: the correction is intrinsically short-ranged.

Pauli-weight compressibility

Locality is not just leading-order: almost all of the generator's Frobenius mass sits in low-weight terms. At $n=5$, $q=2$, $\delta t=0.1$, weight $\le 3$ already accounts for $99.997\%$ of the squared-coefficient mass. Truncating to the Pauli projection $\Pi_w K_q$ is therefore the unique Frobenius-optimal way to compress the generator.

Learned residual model

Because the target is local, it can be learned from local data. A translation-equivariant MLP (12 inputs → two width-64 tanh layers → 48 weight-$\le3$ coefficients, with weights shared across sites and chain lengths) maps local couplings to $\delta t^{-3}$-scaled coefficients. It never sees the dense $2^n$ propagator, which is precisely what lets it transfer to sizes its training never reached.

Contributions & Novelty

What is new, stated precisely.

  • An exact, optimal correction target. $R_q=US_q^\dagger$ is the unique unitary left correction and the global minimizer of the one-step error in every unitarily invariant norm — there is nothing arbitrary about what the network is asked to learn.
  • Stability under approximation. An approximate residual still controls global error: it is bounded by $\le r\eta$ (and by $\le r\lVert K_q-\widehat K_q\rVert_2$), and the linear-in-$r$ rate is proven order-optimal, so learning error cannot amplify uncontrollably across steps.
  • Provably optimal, provably local compression. The Pauli projection is the unique optimal compressed generator, and for the TFIM the leading Strang generator carries weight $\le3$ — together making a small, fixed coefficient set sufficient.
  • An oracle-free residual learner (the central advance). A single network trains on local patches without a dense $2^n$ oracle, transfers from $n=4,5$ to $n\le10$ at $R^2=0.9998$, cuts the Strang error by $\sim\!38\text{--}46\times$, and beats leading-order BCH by >10× exactly where BCH breaks down — all inside the $r\eta$ budget.
  • Full reproducibility. Deterministic dense-matrix experiments, figures, tables, and code recompute every value from first principles.
Results

A network trained on small chains corrects ones it never saw.

The transfer test is the decisive one. On the disordered TFIM ($J_i,h_i\sim\mathcal U[0.5,1.5]$, $t=1$, $\delta t=0.1$), the oracle-free network is trained only on $n=4,5$; every size $n\ge6$ is absent from training. Global spectral-norm error is reported as the mean $\pm$ one standard deviation over the disordered chains, and the reduction factor is $\epsilon_{\mathrm{Strang}}/\epsilon_{\mathrm{learned}}$.

Finite-precision caveat

The exact residual theorem is an algebraic identity, but floating-point arithmetic imposes a floor on what can be measured. The $q=8$ baseline at $\delta t=0.1$ already approaches round-off, so the oracle residual may match or even marginally exceed it. Values near $10^{-13}$–$10^{-14}$ reflect the double-precision floor, not physical error. The compression results are read off well above round-off (for example, the $w=3,4$ rows), where the comparison is meaningful.

Conclusion & Impact

An exactly optimal target, made safe to approximate — and learned without ever evaluating it.

Meaning

Trotter compilation becomes a learning problem that arrives with its own proof. A single per-site network, trained on small systems from oracle-free patches, corrects systems $32\times$ larger in Hilbert-space dimension, outperforms the analytic leading-order correction precisely where that correction fails, and stays within a closed-form $r\eta$ error budget throughout.

Transparent limitations

Oracle RGTC requires $U(\delta t)$ and is as costly as exact dense simulation. The oracle-free learned residual removes the dense $2^n$ oracle from training, while evaluation here remains dense (up to ten qubits), the templates are TFIM-specific and leading-order, the results use double precision, and native-gate synthesis is left to future work. These define the next steps and stand alongside the present contribution.
