\title{The Trotter residual as an inference object: a learnable, certified Lie-algebraic spectral truncation}
\author{Molena Huynh}
\email{molena.huynh@jmp.com}
\affiliation{North Carolina State University}

\begin{abstract}
A quantum computer is a stochastic device one can only interrogate by sampling, so almost everything one wants from it---an expectation value, a reconstructed state, a hardware fidelity---is an estimation or inference problem under sampling noise. The finite-step error of a product formula is usually treated as the rare deterministic exception, an analytic object bounded a priori rather than inferred. We show it is better cast as the same kind of object handled well: a structured, compressible, \emph{learnable} operator that can be \emph{certified} instead of estimated, and we use it to advance the Trotter--Suzuki accuracy--cost frontier. The residual factor $R_q=U(\dt)S_q(\dt)^\dagger$ of an order-$q$ step is the unique unitary correction to the exact propagator, optimal in every unitarily invariant norm; projecting its Hermitian generator onto the Pauli-weight filtration of the Lie algebra of Hermitian operators is the unique Frobenius-optimal compression---a \emph{Lie-algebraic spectral truncation} with one accuracy knob. We prove this truncation converges \emph{geometrically} in the level, with a measured per-level error reduction of $4.5$--$5.8\times10^{2}$, and carries an a priori certificate---a deterministic guarantee in place of a sampled confidence interval---that fixes the truncation bandwidth from the target accuracy alone, before any simulation. Compiled into mutually-commuting two-qubit-gate layers, a spectrally-truncated fourth-order correction reaches global spectral-norm errors between standard sixth- and eighth-order Suzuki formulas at $4.2$--$5.2\times$ fewer two-qubit gates than the cheapest standard formula of equal accuracy ($n=5$--$7$), placing new Pareto-optimal points on the frontier. The correction is \emph{inferred}, not measured: one translation-equivariant network, trained offline on four- and five-qubit chains without the dense $2^n$ propagator, transfers as an estimator to ten qubits ($R^2=0.9999$). The gain is a new accuracy regime reachable at a fraction of the next order's cost, not a uniform cost reduction, and every reported number is an exact, deterministic, reproducible computation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

From where a user sits, a quantum computer is a stochastic device interrogated only by sampling: the Born rule returns one bitstring per run, so every quantity of interest is reconstructed from repeated shots under sampling noise. Statistics is therefore not peripheral to quantum computing but structural to it, and most of the useful work is some form of estimation or inference. Estimating an observable---the energy in a variational eigensolver or an optimization circuit---is an estimation problem with a variance and a sample-complexity trade-off; reconstructing a state or process is statistical inference from measurement counts~\cite{gross2010cs}; certifying that hardware works, through randomized benchmarking~\cite{magesan2011rb} and the cross-entropy benchmarking behind the first claims of quantum advantage~\cite{arute2019}, is estimation joined to hypothesis testing; decoding an error from a syndrome is Bayesian inference, the maximum-likelihood coset problem at the heart of fault tolerance~\cite{dennis2002}; monitoring calibration drift is a change-point problem~\cite{proctor2020drift}; and newer staples such as classical shadows are pure randomized-measurement statistics~\cite{huang2020shadows}. Against this backdrop the finite-step error of a digital simulation is conspicuous: it is one of the few quantities normally treated deterministically---bounded a priori by an analytic argument, never inferred from data. This paper takes that error seriously as an inference object and shows the exception is only apparent. The right way to handle the product-formula residual is not to estimate it shot by shot, which would amount to tomographing a correction channel, but to expose its structure so that it can be \emph{learned} where learning is cheap and \emph{certified} where a guarantee is available---trading a sampled confidence interval for a deterministic one.

Digital Hamiltonian simulation implements $U(t)=\exp(-iHt)$ for a many-body Hamiltonian $H$, and product formulas remain its most transparent and hardware-aligned route. They also impose a hard trade-off: a smaller error demands a higher-order formula and many more gates, so accuracy is bought in coarse, expensive steps in the product-formula order. We close part of that gap by correcting the finite-step error directly, advancing the accuracy--cost frontier of the formula one already runs. Product formulas trace to Lloyd's observation that local quantum systems are efficiently simulable~\cite{lloyd1996} and to Trotter's theorem~\cite{trotter1959}; higher-order recursive splittings were developed by Suzuki~\cite{suzuki1990,suzuki1991,suzuki1993} and by related symplectic-composition methods~\cite{yoshida1990,forest1990,chin1997,hatano2005,wiebe2010}. They underlie early quantum simulation proposals~\cite{abrams1997,sornborger1999,ortiz2001}, phase-estimation workflows~\cite{kitaev1997,kitaev2002,abrams1999,cleve1998}, and practical resource estimates in quantum chemistry and lattice simulation~\cite{aspuruguzik2005,wecker2014,wecker2015,babbush2015,reiher2017,kivlichan2018,haah2021}.

The current theory of product-formula simulation is much sharper than the original asymptotic picture. Commutator-sensitive bounds explain when product formulas outperform worst-case estimates~\cite{childs2021,layden2022,zhuk2023}, locality improves lattice-simulation scaling~\cite{childs2019lattice,lieb1972,tran2020}, and the cost of implementing Trotter steps has itself become an object of complexity analysis~\cite{low2023pra}. At the same time, product formulas compete with multi-product formulas~\cite{childs2012mpf}, truncated Taylor methods~\cite{berry2015prl}, quantum signal processing and qubitization~\cite{berry2007,berry2014,low2017qsp,low2019qubitization}, randomized formulas~\cite{campbell2019,ouyang2020,childs2022,chen2022a}, Magnus-expansion algorithms~\cite{magnus1954,blanes2009,sharma2024,fang2025}, variational fast-forwarding~\cite{cirstoiu2020}, and Lie-algebraic decomposition or compilation approaches~\cite{kokcu2022,steckmann2023,wu2023,zeier2011}. These methods are complementary, but they all confront the same conceptual object: the finite-step defect between the desired unitary and the implemented unitary.

This work builds most directly on the Hamiltonian-simulation neighborhood of the
second-order Zassenhaus factorization and the associated root-activity
complexity bounds~\cite{nguyen2025zassenhaus,jing2026complexity}. We adopt the
second-order Zassenhaus generator as an analytic prior on top of which a network
learns a structured residual correction, and we prove how that correction's
approximation error propagates across steps. Rather than change the product
formula or prove a representation-theoretic lower bound, we compress and
faithfully compile the residual generator of a \emph{higher-order} step, and this
moves the product-formula accuracy--cost frontier itself
(Sec.~\ref{sec:frontier}), under guarantees inherited from the analytic prior and
the locality of the defect.

We isolate that defect as an operator to be studied, bounded, compressed, and ultimately compiled. For a product-formula step $S_q(\dt)$ of order $q$, define
\begin{equation}
    R_q(\dt)=U(\dt)S_q(\dt)^\dagger,
    \label{eq:intro-residual}
\end{equation}
where $U(\dt)=\exp(-iH\dt)$. The corrected step $R_q(\dt)S_q(\dt)$ is exactly $U(\dt)$ in exact arithmetic. This identity is simple; we turn it into a rigorous benchmark and a nontrivial approximation problem. The residual is unique, unitary, norm-optimal, and stable under approximation. Its Hermitian generator, defined by $R_q(\dt)=\exp[-iK_q(\dt)]$, is small for small $\dt$ and inherits local commutator structure from the product-formula defect. Once $K_q$ is exposed, a natural compressed implementation problem emerges: choose an implementable operator subspace $\cB$, project or learn $K_q$ inside that subspace, and implement $\exp[-i\Pi_{\cB}K_q]S_q$ instead of $S_q$.

We distinguish two modes of residual-generator Trotter compilation (\rgtc{}). \emph{Oracle \rgtc{}} computes $R_q$ from the exact dense matrix exponential; it is not scalable and serves to establish the target and the best possible one-step left correction. \emph{Compressed \rgtc{}} uses a Hermitian generator $\widehat K_q$ from a restricted family to form $\widehat R_q=\exp(-i\widehat K_q)$, and is the deployable object: it can be instantiated by Baker--Campbell--Hausdorff (BCH) truncation, Pauli-weight projection, variational circuit compilation, operator learning, or GPU-accelerated offline fitting. The exact identity $US_q^\dagger S_q=U$ is trivial on its own; the substance is what happens when the residual is approximated, and we show that structured approximations work. We then go further: we compile the compressed mode into gates and show that, applied to an already-high-order step, it advances the product-formula error--cost frontier (Sec.~\ref{sec:frontier}), and we instantiate it as a \emph{learned} residual generator that, on disordered Ising chains, transfers from small systems to larger ones it never saw, under the proven stability guarantee. The compressed-and-compiled mode is the central practical advance; the learned residual generator makes such corrections obtainable without the dense propagator; and the exact theory makes both principled.

Our contributions are as follows. (i) We formalize \rgtc{} and prove exact residual factorization, uniqueness, and global norm optimality among all left corrections for every unitarily invariant norm. (ii) We prove multi-step stability theorems for approximate residuals and generator approximations, giving explicit $r\eta$ and $r\norm{K_q-\widehat K_q}_2$ error targets, and prove the linear-in-$r$ estimate is order-optimal. (iii) We recast Pauli-weight projection of the residual generator as a \emph{Lie-algebraic spectral truncation}---the unique Frobenius-optimal compressed generator (Theorem~\ref{thm:pauli-projection})---whose level $w$ interpolates from no correction ($w=0$) to the exact generator ($w=n$), the Hamiltonian-simulation counterpart of the operator-algebraic spectral truncations recently introduced in kernel learning~\cite{hashimoto2024spectral,hashimoto2023rkhm}. (iv) We prove the truncation converges \emph{geometrically} in the level---each excluded weight level carries residual content of strictly higher order in $\dt$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate})---with a measured per-level reduction of $4.5$--$5.8\times10^{2}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:spectral}a, Table~\ref{tab:spectral-rate}). (v) We prove the a priori certificate $r\norm{K_q-\Pi_wK_q}_2$ is a valid global error bound at every level (Fig.~\ref{fig:spectral}c), so it selects the minimal truncation bandwidth from the target accuracy \emph{before} any simulation (Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}). (vi) We prove residual locality holds at every order: the leading order-$q$ residual generator of the open-boundary transverse-field Ising model (\tfim{}) has Pauli weight at most $q+2$, with an effective threshold $\approx\lceil q/2\rceil+2$ (Table~\ref{tab:order-generality}), so higher-order corrections stay compressible. (vii) We prove and verify that the truncated correction \emph{compiles faithfully} into gates: because $K_q=O(\dt^{q+1})$, a first-order product over mutually-commuting Pauli-rotation layers adds only $O(\dt^{2(q+1)})$ error, negligible for $q\ge4$ but \emph{not} for $q=2$, and a \emph{symmetric inner compilation} removes this structural floor at every order ($O(\dt^{3(q+1)})$ error, Theorem~\ref{thm:symmetric-compilation})---e.g.\ lowering the compiled $n=6$, $q=2$, $w\le4$ error from $2.18\times10^{-5}$ to $3.76\times10^{-7}$ (Table~\ref{tab:faithful-compilation}). (viii)~This yields the central result: a genuine advance of the product-formula error--cost frontier. Under a standard two-qubit-gate (CNOT) cost model, spectrally-truncated fourth-order corrections reach global spectral-norm errors between standard sixth- and eighth-order Suzuki formulas at $4.2$--$5.2\times$ fewer two-qubit gates than the cheapest standard formula of equal accuracy ($n=5$--$7$), placing new Pareto-optimal points on the frontier (Fig.~\ref{fig:frontier}). (ix) We instantiate the learned mode: a single translation-equivariant network predicts the step-size-conditioned weight-$\leq3$ coefficients of $K_2$ from local couplings of a disordered \tfim{}, transfers from four- and five-qubit training chains to ten qubits ($R^2=0.9999$ on held-out sizes), cuts the uncorrected Strang error by $58$--$87\times$, is trained \emph{without any dense $2^n$ oracle} from fixed-size local patches, and beats the analytic leading-order correction where the latter fails---all under the proven $r\eta$ stability bound. (x) We show the mechanism extends from the \tfim{} to the XXZ/Heisenberg chain, with a larger weight threshold (weight $\approx5$, a $54\times$ reduction at $w\leq5$; Table~\ref{tab:xxz}). (xi) We provide deterministic dense-matrix experiments, figures, tables, and code that recompute every reported number from first principles. Figure~\ref{fig:schematic} summarizes the pipeline, from the local Hamiltonian to the certified, compiled, frontier-advancing correction.

Beyond quantum simulation, the central finding is of methodological interest to machine learning for the physical sciences: the truncation error of a numerical integrator, usually treated as an analytic object to be bounded, is here a \emph{learnable} operator---geometrically local, conditioned on the discretization step, and transferable across system size---with its approximation quality controlled \emph{a priori} by a proven stability bound rather than validated only \emph{a posteriori}. The enabling compression is algebraic: the correction lives in the Lie algebra of Hermitian operators, and Pauli-weight projection is a \emph{spectral truncation} of that algebra to a finite-dimensional, symmetry-respecting hypothesis class indexed by an interaction-range knob, in direct analogy with the operator-algebraic spectral truncations that interpolate a locality knob in C$^\ast$-algebraic kernel learning~\cite{hashimoto2024spectral,hashimoto2023rkhm}. The analogy is exact at the level of the construction---a single parameter contracting a noncommutative algebra to a tractable, structure-respecting subspace---but the simulation setting supplies three guarantees the kernel construction does not: a quantitative \emph{geometric convergence rate} in the level rather than mere convergence (Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate}); an \emph{a priori dynamical certificate} that turns the truncation level into a function of the target accuracy (Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}), whose own gate-level realization we repair by a symmetric compilation (Theorem~\ref{thm:symmetric-compilation}), the analogue of the kernel construction's positivity-restoring smoothing; and a measurable downstream payoff---an advance of the algorithm's accuracy--cost frontier. This places the work within the broader programme of structure-preserving, physics-constrained operator learning, where a restricted, symmetry-respecting hypothesis class is what makes generalization both data-efficient and certifiable.

This manuscript is the ninth in a single-author programme that follows one statistical thread through quantum task after quantum task: each entry isolates a quantity one would naively estimate by sampling and replaces the estimate, where structure permits, by a learned surrogate or an a priori guarantee. The programme begins with graph-conditioned trust regions for query-efficient quantum approximate optimization~\cite{huynh2026gctr}---training a variational circuit is stochastic optimization over an objective one can only estimate---and their uncertainty-calibrated extension~\cite{huynh2026gctrcalpro}, quantifies the measurement cost, that is the sample complexity, of warm-started low-depth schedules~\cite{huynh2026meascost}, and certifies the associated query budgets~\cite{huynh2026certqb}. It then establishes topology-conditioned parameter transfer for budgeted graph optimization~\cite{huynh2026paramxferB}---inference that generalizes across instances---identifies operator-spectral truncated priors as the object that makes such transfer query-efficient~\cite{huynh2026ostqaoa}, and extends the spectral-truncation graph kernels beyond depth one~\cite{huynh2026topoqaoa}. The most recent entry carries the truncation into product-formula dynamics, cancelling the leading Trotter error by commutator-graph scheduling at zero gate overhead~\cite{huynh2026topoham}. The present work completes that move: rather than scheduling away the leading error term, it treats the entire finite-step residual as the operator to be spectrally truncated, filtered by Pauli weight, and compiled, so the same locality-indexed contraction that selected variational templates now selects the simulation correction and, through the certificate of Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}, fixes its bandwidth a priori. The simulation error is the programme's purest instance of the thesis read in reverse: a quantity one could estimate by sampling is instead structured until it can be certified, with learning held in reserve for exactly the part---the per-site generator coefficients---where a guarantee runs out. The same operator-spectral / spectral-truncation toolkit that the programme has built up across variational optimization and product-formula dynamics is what this manuscript carries into Hamiltonian simulation, connected throughout by its shared locality-indexed truncation.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Hamiltonian model and product-formula baselines}

We first fix the Hamiltonian model, the product-formula steps, and the error metric used throughout. Let $\cH\cong\mathbb{C}^d$ and let $H=H^\dagger\in\mathbb{C}^{d\times d}$, with exact time-$t$ propagator $U(t)=e^{-iHt}$. We assume a decomposition $H=A+B$ with $A=A^\dagger$ and $B=B^\dagger$ each easier to exponentiate than their sum; this two-term setting covers the standard even-odd or diagonal-transverse splittings used in many spin models, and generalizes directly to $m$ terms. The global $r$-step baseline error at $t=r\dt$ is
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon_{T,q}(t;r)=\norm{U(t)-S_q(\dt)^r}_2.
    \label{eq:baseline-error}
\end{equation}
All numerical results use the spectral norm because it is basis independent, operationally meaningful for worst-case state-vector error, and directly computable from dense singular values for the small systems considered here. The first-order Lie--Trotter step is $S_1(\dt)=e^{-iB\dt}e^{-iA\dt}$, the symmetric second-order Strang step is $S_2(\dt)=e^{-iB\dt/2}e^{-iA\dt}e^{-iB\dt/2}$, and higher even orders are generated by the Suzuki recursion (Appendix~\ref{app:methods}); the elementary factor counts are $2,3,15,75,375$ for $q=1,2,4,6,8$. The benchmark Hamiltonian is the open-boundary \tfim{}
\begin{equation}
    H=A+B,\qquad A=J\sum_{j=1}^{n-1}Z_jZ_{j+1},\qquad B=h\sum_{j=1}^{n}X_j,
    \label{eq:tfim}
\end{equation}
a standard many-body benchmark with a clear two-term splitting~\cite{sachdev2011,cerveralierta2018}, small enough for exact dense simulation at $n\leq6$ while still exhibiting noncommuting local structure. It is a controlled laboratory for verifying theorems and stress-testing residual compression, and the same analysis can be repeated on electronic-structure Hamiltonians after a fermion-to-qubit transformation~\cite{bravyi2002,whitfield2011,seeley2012,verstraete2005}, Hubbard models~\cite{cade2020}, lattice field theories~\cite{jordan2012}, or quantum-chemistry instances used in fault-tolerant resource analyses~\cite{babbush2018,babbush2019}.

\subsection{Fixed-time oracle benchmark}

Table~\ref{tab:error-summary} reports the fixed-time dense benchmark. For $q=1,2,4,6$, oracle \rgtc{} reaches the $10^{-15}$--$10^{-14}$ range while baselines range from $10^{-1}$ down to $10^{-9}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:fixed-time}). For $q=8$, the baseline itself is already close to round-off, and the oracle construction adds additional dense multiplications; the observed oracle error is therefore of the same order as, and sometimes slightly larger than, the baseline round-off. This is a finite-precision artifact, not a contradiction of Theorem~\ref{thm:factorization}.

\subsection{Projected residual compilation}

Weight-truncated compression of the residual sharpens by orders of magnitude once the truncation reaches the support of the leading defect. The non-oracle experiment for $n=5$, $q=2$, $J=h=1$, $t=1$, and $r=10$ (Table~\ref{tab:projected-summary}; Fig.~\ref{edfig:projected}) shows that weight zero applies essentially no correction, weight one gives a small improvement, and weight two is slightly worse than the baseline; not every truncated correction is automatically useful. Weight three then reduces the error by a factor of $89.56$, consistent with Theorem~\ref{thm:tfim-support}, and weight four reduces it by a factor of $7.48\times10^4$. Varying total time for $n=4$, $q=2$, $r=10$ (Fig.~\ref{edfig:timesweep}), the $w\leq3$ projected residual remains below the baseline throughout the sampled times, while the $w\leq2$ projection is less reliable: compression must respect the commutator support of the leading defect.

\subsection{Generator compressibility and order scaling}

The residual generator concentrates its Pauli-coefficient mass at low weight. For the $n=5$, $q=2$ residual generator at $\dt=0.1$ (Fig.~\ref{edfig:genstruct}a), the cumulative squared coefficient mass shows that most of the Frobenius mass accumulates by weight three, while near-exact reconstruction requires weights four and five at the observed double-precision level. The residual-generator norm $\norm{K_q(\dt)}_2$ versus $\dt$ for several orders (Fig.~\ref{edfig:genstruct}b) decreases with order: higher-order product formulas yield smaller residual generators, as expected from Theorem~\ref{thm:small-step}.

\subsection{Residual locality generalizes to every order}
\label{sec:order-generality}

The compression of Sec.~\ref{sec:results} is not special to the second-order step. We computed the exact residual generator $K_q$ for base orders $q=2,4,6$ and applied the same Pauli-weight truncation $\Pi_w$ (Table~\ref{tab:order-generality}). At each order the projected residual collapses by many orders of magnitude once $w$ reaches a model-set threshold, and that threshold grows only slowly with the order: the dominant drop appears at weight $3$ for $q=2$, weight $4$--$5$ for $q=4$, and weight $5$--$6$ for $q=6$. This is exactly the behaviour predicted by the order-generality locality theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality}): the leading order-$q$ residual generator is a sum of degree-$(q{+}1)$ nested commutators of geometrically local terms and is therefore supported on Pauli weight at most $q+2$, while cancellations in the symmetric formula push the \emph{effective} threshold even lower, to $\approx\lceil q/2\rceil+2$. The practical consequence is direct: higher-order product formulas have residual generators that are \emph{still} low-weight and hence still compressible and compilable, which makes the frontier advance below possible.

\subsection{Spectral truncation of the residual generator: convergence rate and certified bandwidth}
\label{sec:spectral}

The compression of the previous sections is, formally, a \emph{spectral truncation} of the Lie algebra of Hermitian operators: the Pauli-weight subspaces $\cB_0\subseteq\cB_1\subseteq\cdots\subseteq\cB_n=\mathsf{Herm}(d)$ filter that algebra by interaction range, and projecting the residual generator onto $\cB_w$ contracts a noncommutative algebra to a tractable, symmetry-respecting subspace indexed by a single level $w$ (Definition~\ref{def:spectral-truncation}). This is the Hamiltonian-simulation counterpart of the operator-algebraic spectral truncations of Refs.~\cite{hashimoto2024spectral,hashimoto2023rkhm}, and we show it carries two guarantees that go beyond that construction: a quantitative convergence \emph{rate}, and an a priori certificate that selects the truncation level from the target accuracy.

\paragraph{Geometric convergence rate.} The truncated-residual global error converges \emph{geometrically} in the level, not merely in a limit (Fig.~\ref{fig:spectral}a, Table~\ref{tab:spectral-rate}). For the open-boundary \tfim{} at $n=6$, $\dt=0.1$, once $w$ reaches the effective threshold $w_0=\lceil q/2\rceil+2$ each additional retained weight level multiplies the corrected error by a measured factor $449\times$ for $q=2$ and $576\times$ for $q=4$ (least-squares fit of $\log_{10}$ error versus $w$). Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate} explains and bounds this: each excluded weight level carries residual content of strictly higher order in $\dt$ (a consequence of the symmetric formula's odd-power expansion and the locality--degree bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality}), so the truncation tail---and with it the corrected error, through the certificate of Corollary~\ref{cor:projected-certificate}---decays geometrically in the level. The provable per-level factor is at least $\dt^{-1}=10$; the measured factors ($449\times$ at $q=2$, $576\times$ at $q=4$) are far steeper, because the same symmetric cancellations that lower the effective locality threshold (Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality}, Remark) make each retained level absorb content roughly two powers of $\dt$ smaller. The geometric rate, rather than mere convergence, is what turns the truncation level into a usable accuracy dial.

\paragraph{Certificate-driven bandwidth selection.} The truncation bandwidth needed for a target accuracy $\epsilon$ can be read off \emph{before} paying for any simulation. Every level carries the a priori bound $\norm{\widehat G_{q,w}^{\,r}-U(t)}_2\leq r\norm{K_q-\Pi_wK_q}_2$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:projected-certificate}), whose right-hand side is computable from the generator without simulating the dynamics, so one selects the smallest $w$ with $r\norm{K_q-\Pi_wK_q}_2\leq\epsilon$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}). On the $n=6$, $q=2$ benchmark this selection is both valid and tight: the certificate is an upper bound on the achieved error at every level (Fig.~\ref{fig:spectral}c), and it selects $w^\star=4$ for $\epsilon=10^{-4}$ (certificate $r\tau_{4}=3.7\times10^{-7}\leq\epsilon$; achieved error $3.65\times10^{-7}$) and $w^\star=5$ for $\epsilon=10^{-8}$ (certificate $1.1\times10^{-9}$; achieved $1.10\times10^{-9}$); here the certificate is unusually tight, but in general it is a guaranteed upper bound, not an equality. The geometric rate makes this map cheap: the required bandwidth grows only logarithmically in $1/\epsilon$. The operator-algebraic spectral truncation has no analogue of this accuracy-to-parameter map, because its guarantee is structural (it preserves positive-definiteness) rather than a quantitative bound on a downstream quantity.

\subsection{Faithful compilation and the error--cost frontier}
\label{sec:frontier}

We turn the projected residual from an operator into an algorithm: we compile $\exp(-i\Pi_wK_q)$ into gates and count those gates exactly. Because $\Pi_wK_q$ is a sum of geometrically local Pauli strings, it decomposes into a small number of mutually-commuting layers, each implementable as one elementary exponential, so the whole correction is realized by a first-order product over those layers. Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation} bounds the extra error of this inner product formula by $O(\dt^{2(q+1)})$ per step, and this budget decides whether the correction helps. At second order the correction is not small enough: the compiled $w\leq4$ correction stalls at $2.18\times10^{-5}$ (Table~\ref{tab:frontier}) while the dense oracle reaches $3.65\times10^{-7}$ (Table~\ref{tab:faithful-compilation})---a gap we close with the symmetric compilation below. At fourth order the correction is tiny ($K_4=O(\dt^5)$), so the compiled correction faithfully tracks the oracle: at $n=6$ the compiled $w\leq5$ correction reaches $7.95\times10^{-10}$ against the oracle's $4.19\times10^{-10}$.

\paragraph{A symmetric inner compilation repairs the floor at every order.} The $q=2$ stall is not intrinsic to the compressed correction---it reaches $3.65\times10^{-7}$ as a dense operator---but to the \emph{first-order} inner product over the commuting layers, whose splitting error is $O(\dt^{2(q+1)})$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation}). Replacing it by a \emph{symmetric} (Strang-ordered) inner product, $\prod_i e^{-iL_i/2}\prod_i e^{-iL_i/2}$ over the layers in reverse, cancels the leading splitting commutator and lowers the inner error to $O(\dt^{3(q+1)})$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:symmetric-compilation}), at the cost of applying each layer twice (the correction's two-qubit-gate count roughly doubles, with the middle layer merged). This recovers the truncated-oracle accuracy at \emph{every} base order, including the $q=2$ case where the first-order compile fails: at $n=6$, $q=2$, $w\le4$ the symmetric compile reaches $3.76\times10^{-7}$, matching the dense oracle $3.65\times10^{-7}$ and improving the first-order compile by $58\times$, while at $q=4$ the first-order compile is already faithful and cheaper (Fig.~\ref{fig:spectral}b, Table~\ref{tab:faithful-compilation}). This is the simulation analogue of the smoothing that restores positive-definiteness in operator-algebraic spectral truncation~\cite{hashimoto2024spectral}: a structurally-motivated reordering that repairs the naive realization of the truncated object. The headline frontier advance below uses the cheaper first-order compile, which is already faithful at $q=4$; the symmetric compile is what makes the compress--then--compile pipeline faithful \emph{universally}, closing the low-order gap.

\input{tables/faithful_compilation.tex}

We adopt the standard two-qubit-gate (CNOT) cost model (Appendix~\ref{app:numerics}): a \tfim{} $ZZ$-rotation layer costs $2(n-1)$ CNOTs, the transverse-field layer is single-qubit (free), and a weight-$w$ Pauli rotation costs $2(w-1)$ CNOTs. Figure~\ref{fig:frontier} and Table~\ref{tab:frontier} plot every method on the resulting error--cost plane. The residual-corrected fourth-order step is \emph{Pareto-optimal}: at $n=6$ it attains $7.95\times10^{-10}$ at $274$ CNOTs per step, an accuracy that lies in the gap between standard sixth order ($3.50\times10^{-9}$, $250$ CNOTs) and eighth order ($1.71\times10^{-13}$, $1250$ CNOTs). The only standard Suzuki formula more accurate is eighth order, which costs $4.6\times$ more two-qubit gates; equivalently, to reach this accuracy with a standard product formula one must jump to eighth order and pay $1250$ CNOTs, whereas the corrected fourth-order step pays $274$. The advantage holds across size: the cheapest standard formula matching the corrected accuracy costs $5.2\times$, $4.6\times$, and $4.2\times$ more two-qubit gates at $n=5,6,7$ respectively. The corrected second-order points (Table~\ref{tab:frontier}) are \emph{not} on the frontier---they are dominated by simply using a higher order---so the advance is specific to correcting an already-high order, exactly where the faithful-compilation budget holds.

We state the scope precisely. This is \emph{not} a uniform cost reduction: the correction's two-qubit-gate count grows slightly faster with $n$ than the sixth-order step, so the corrected fourth-order step is $3.7$--$4.7\times$ \emph{more accurate} than sixth order at comparable ($0.96$--$1.19\times$) cost, but it is not cheaper than sixth order. The genuine advance is the new accuracy regime---between consecutive standard orders---made reachable at a fraction of the next order's cost, i.e.\ new Pareto-optimal points on a frontier that was previously a coarse staircase in the product-formula order.

\paragraph{The frontier correction is local and persists with size.} The Pareto-optimal corrected step is built \emph{entirely} from the complete weight-$\leq5$ \emph{local} template set---it uses no global or long-range Pauli content---and the advantage persists to the largest dense-verifiable size (Table~\ref{tab:oracle-free-q4}): the purely-local correction reaches $1.28\times10^{-9}$ at $470$ CNOTs per step at $n=8$ ($3.7\times$ fewer two-qubit gates than the matching standard eighth order) and $2.03\times10^{-9}$ at $650$ CNOTs at $n=10$ ($3.5\times$ saving). The frontier advance is therefore not a small-system artifact and requires only geometrically local operators; by Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality} the correction's coefficients are, in principle, obtainable from a fixed size-independent reference patch rather than the dense propagator. One caveat bounds a fully oracle-free realization: tiling those coefficients from a small ($8$-qubit) patch is not yet bulk-converged for the $q=4$ generator (the tiled $n=10$ error is $\sim6.6\times10^{-8}$, an order of magnitude above the direct local $2.0\times10^{-9}$), so a fully oracle-free realization \emph{at frontier accuracy} requires a larger---still size-independent---patch or the learned per-site map demonstrated at second order (Sec.~\ref{sec:learned}).

\subsection{Generality beyond the transverse-field Ising model}
\label{sec:xxz}

The locality--compression mechanism is not confined to the \tfim{}. On the XXZ chain $H=\sum_i J_{xy}(X_iX_{i+1}+Y_iY_{i+1})+J_z Z_iZ_{i+1}+\sum_i hZ_i$ with an even--odd bond splitting, the same projected-residual construction again collapses the error once the retained weight is large enough (Table~\ref{tab:xxz}). The threshold exceeds that of the \tfim{}. Because both split terms are two-local, nested commutators grow support faster, so the second-order residual needs weight $\approx5$ (a $54\times$ reduction at $w\leq5$) rather than weight $3$, and the fourth-order residual compresses only at still higher weight on $n=6$. This is the expected behaviour: the method's compressibility tracks the locality of the chosen splitting, and it delineates where residual correction is most effective---splittings, like the \tfim{}'s, in which one term is strictly lower-range.

\subsection{Inferring the residual generator: a transferable estimator learned without the propagator}
\label{sec:learned}

This is the one place in the paper where genuine estimation enters, and it enters on favorable terms. Everywhere else the residual is computed or certified; here its coefficients are \emph{inferred} from data by a surrogate model---exactly the kind of stochastic, sample-based learning that the rest of quantum computing cannot avoid---except that this inference is data-efficient, transfers across system size, and inherits the manuscript's a priori error bound rather than resting on a posteriori validation alone. The locality theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:tfim-support}) turns residual compilation into a well-posed learning problem. Because the leading Strang residual generator is supported on geometrically local Pauli strings of weight at most three, the map from a Hamiltonian to its residual coefficients is itself local: the coefficient of a string supported on a few neighboring sites depends only on the couplings in that neighborhood. A single function applied at every site can therefore reconstruct the whole generator, and the \emph{same} function applies to a chain of any length. This is the inductive bias that allows a model trained on small chains to correct larger ones.

\paragraph{Setup.} We use the disordered open-boundary \tfim{} $H=\sum_i J_iZ_iZ_{i+1}+\sum_i h_iX_i$ with site-dependent couplings $J_i,h_i$ drawn uniformly from $[0.5,1.5]$. At each site we parameterize the residual generator by the $48$ weight-$\leq3$ Pauli templates whose support lies within a three-site window anchored there. A single translation-equivariant multilayer perceptron ($12$ inputs---$11$ local couplings plus the step size $\dt$---through two width-$64$ \texttt{tanh} layers to $48$ coefficients, with weights shared across all sites and all chain lengths) predicts the $\dt^{-3}$-scaled coefficients, and the correction is rebuilt as $\dt^3$ times the network output. Training draws $220$ disordered realizations at each of $n=4$ and $n=5$ with $\dt$ sampled in $[0.05,0.30]$, so one network spans a range of step sizes. Sparse Pauli arithmetic lets us reconstruct and evaluate generators up to $n=10$ (a $1024$-dimensional Hilbert space) without materializing dense Pauli matrices. We also compare against the parameter-free \emph{leading-order BCH} correction $\dt^3K_2^{(3)}$, whose local coefficients we read off analytically from small patches. We use this analytic leading-order generator---equivalently the second-order Zassenhaus term of Ref.~\cite{nguyen2025zassenhaus}---as a \emph{prior}: the network predicts only the residual coefficients \emph{beyond} it (delta learning), and the prior is added back at inference. A prior-free ablation that instead learns the full generator directly isolates the prior's contribution.

\paragraph{Two label sources.} We train the network two ways. \emph{Oracle labels} come from the exact global residual generator computed by dense matrix logarithm at the (small) training sizes. \emph{Oracle-free labels} come instead from the residual generator of a fixed-size local patch (radius two around each three-site support, at most seven qubits), so no dense $2^n$ object ever enters training. At inference either network sees only local couplings---never the exact propagator $U(\dt)$. Figure~\ref{fig:learned} reports six findings.

\emph{Locality is exact.} Reconstructing the oracle generator from the contiguous local templates and from \emph{all} weight-$\leq3$ Pauli strings (any positions) gives identical global error at every size from $n=4$ to $n=10$, numerically confirming Theorem~\ref{thm:tfim-support}: the local parameterization loses no accuracy.

\emph{The coefficients generalize across size.} On held-out sizes $n=6,\dots,10$, absent from training, the oracle-free network's predicted-versus-exact coefficient parity has $R^2=0.9999$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:learned}d).

\emph{The correction transfers to much larger systems.} Trained only on $n=4,5$, the same per-site network reduces the global spectral-norm error of the uncorrected Strang step at $t=1$ (mean over disordered chains) from $1.05\times10^{-2}$ to $1.20\times10^{-4}$ at $n=4$, and from $3.67\times10^{-2}$ to $5.76\times10^{-4}$ at $n=10$---a $58$--$87\times$ reduction, with $n=6,\dots,10$ entirely outside the training set (Fig.~\ref{fig:learned}a; per-size errors, reduction factors, and held-out markers are tabulated in Table~\ref{tab:learned-summary}). It tracks the exact weight-$\leq3$ oracle to within a factor of about $1.2$ to $1.8$, and sits roughly $8\times$ below the leading-order BCH baseline at this step size. Shaded bands in Fig.~\ref{fig:learned} are one standard deviation over disordered realizations, so the separation between the learned correction and both the uncorrected step and the analytic baseline is well outside the run-to-run spread. The linear-in-$r$ growth in Fig.~\ref{fig:learned}c and the size transfer to $n=10$ are exactly what Theorem~\ref{thm:size-transfer} predicts, since the per-site error $\eta$ is independent of $n$ under weight sharing.

\emph{The analytic prior is what makes it accurate.} Learning the residual \emph{beyond} the second-order Zassenhaus generator, rather than the full generator, lowers the transfer error by a further $1.5$--$2.0\times$ at every size (Table~\ref{tab:learned-summary}, column $\epsilon^{\mathrm{no\,prior}}_{\mathrm{learned}}$ versus $\epsilon_{\mathrm{learned}}$). The network then only has to capture the higher-order Zassenhaus terms the analytic prior omits---a smaller and smoother target---which is the single change that moves the learned correction from roughly $40\times$ to $58$--$87\times$ error reduction and tightens the held-out coefficient fit to $R^2=0.9999$. The analytic factorization of Ref.~\cite{nguyen2025zassenhaus} and the learned model thereby reinforce rather than compete.

\emph{Training needs no global oracle.} The oracle-free network, trained only on at-most-seven-qubit patches, matches---and slightly improves on---the oracle-trained network at every size (the two learned curves overlap in Fig.~\ref{fig:learned}a). The dense $2^n$ oracle, the central limitation of the framework, is therefore not required to \emph{learn} the correction.

\emph{Learning beats leading-order BCH as the step grows.} In a single-step sweep at $n=6$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:learned}b), leading-order BCH is accurate at small $\dt$ but degrades rapidly: at $\dt=0.3$ it leaves error $1.11\times10^{-1}$, barely below the uncorrected $1.35\times10^{-1}$, because it captures only the $\dt^3$ term. The learned correction, having seen the working-$\dt$ coefficients, holds at $7.7\times10^{-3}$---about $14\times$ below BCH and within about $30\%$ of the exact oracle. Sweeping the number of Trotter steps $r$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:learned}c), the learned error grows linearly in $r$, exactly as Theorem~\ref{thm:residual-stability} predicts for an approximate residual whose per-step generator error is the constant $\eta$ of that bound; Theorem~\ref{thm:stability-tight} shows this linear-in-$r$ growth is order-optimal and cannot be improved.

\emph{The fair product-formula comparison.} The $58$--$87\times$ reduction reported in this subsection is measured against the \emph{uncorrected second-order Strang step}; for the second-order base step alone, simply raising the product-formula order is more accurate at comparable factor count: at $\dt=0.1$ a standard order-4 step reaches $\sim10^{-5}$ and an order-6 step $\sim10^{-9}$ (Table~\ref{tab:error-summary}), below the $\sim10^{-4}$ learned correction on Strang. The resolution is the central point of this work and is established in Sec.~\ref{sec:frontier}: the residual-correction principle is \emph{not} limited to the second-order step, and when applied to a higher-order base step it advances the product-formula error--cost frontier itself. Because the residual generator stays low-weight at every order (Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality}) and compiles faithfully once the base residual is small (Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation}), a residual-corrected \emph{fourth-order} step reaches accuracies between standard sixth and eighth order at a fraction of the eighth-order two-qubit-gate cost (Fig.~\ref{fig:frontier}). The learned, size-transferable mode of this subsection is the mechanism that makes such corrections obtainable without the dense propagator; it is demonstrated here at second order and extends directly to the higher-order templates by Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality}. The value of the framework is therefore not only the transferable learning principle but the concrete frontier advance it enables.

Two scope statements are important and are revisited in the Discussion. With oracle-free labels the only exact computations are on fixed-size patches whose cost is independent of $n$; evaluation and the leading-order baseline still use dense simulation, and the templates are specific to the \tfim{}. What the experiment establishes is concrete: the residual generator is a learnable, geometrically local, step-size-conditioned object; it can be learned without any global oracle; its learned approximation inherits the manuscript's error guarantees; it outperforms the analytic leading-order correction where that correction fails; and it transfers to systems much larger than those used in training. It does \emph{not} establish that correcting a second-order step is competitive with simply using a higher-order product formula at the same step size, which Table~\ref{tab:error-summary} shows it is not.

\subsection{The residual as a statistical estimand: identifiability, efficiency, and a certified truncation}
\label{sec:inference}

The framework so far computes or certifies the residual generator in software. We close the loop with the introduction's thesis by asking the complementary question: \emph{if} the residual coefficients had to be estimated from measurements---as they must be whenever the correction is characterized on hardware rather than read from a dense propagator---what is the statistically optimal way to do so, and how does the estimation error interact with the deterministic truncation certificate of Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}? This treats the vector of Pauli coefficients of $K_q$ as a parameter $\theta$ of a parametric statistical model, and develops its inference theory: identifiability (Theorem~\ref{thm:identifiability}), the information geometry that lower-bounds any unbiased estimator (Theorem~\ref{thm:crb}), consistency and asymptotic efficiency of the maximum-likelihood estimator (Theorem~\ref{thm:mle}), and---the payoff for this paper---a finite-sample certificate that the \emph{learned, spectrally truncated} residual is within $\epsilon$ of the true residual with confidence $1-\delta$, cleanly splitting the deterministic truncation bias of Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate} from the statistical estimation error (Theorem~\ref{thm:certified-truncation}). The upshot is a precise statement of what ``certified'' buys over ``estimated'': the truncation replaces a $1-\delta$ confidence interval by a deterministic one on exactly the part of the residual that structure has already pinned down, and quantifies the residual sample cost of the part it has not.

\paragraph{A measurement model for the residual coefficients.} Fix the base order $q$, step $\dt$, and truncation level $w$, and let $\{P_1,\dots,P_p\}$ be the real weight-$\leq w$ Pauli basis of $\cB_w$ (Definition~\ref{def:spectral-truncation}), so the truncated generator is $\Pi_wK_q=\sum_{a=1}^{p}\theta_a P_a$ with unknown coefficient vector $\theta=(\theta_1,\dots,\theta_p)\in\mathbb{R}^{p}$; by Theorem~\ref{thm:small-step} the true $\theta^\star$ has $\norm{\theta^\star}$ of order $\dt^{q+1}$. A \emph{design} is a finite set of measurement settings $s=1,\dots,S$, each a pair $(\rho_s,M_s)$ of an input probe state and a Hermitian readout observable with $\norm{M_s}_2\le1$; setting $s$ is repeated over $N_s$ independent shots, $N=\sum_sN_s$ total, with $\pi_s=N_s/N$ the (fixed) allocation. Running the residual channel $R_q(\theta)=e^{-i\sum_a\theta_aP_a}$ once and reading $M_s$ produces an outcome $Y$ with the Born-rule mean
\begin{equation}
    g_s(\theta)=\Tr\!\bigl[M_s\,R_q(\theta)\,\rho_s\,R_q(\theta)^\dagger\bigr],
    \label{eq:born-mean}
\end{equation}
a real-analytic function of $\theta$ bounded in $[-1,1]$; the $N_s$ shots at setting $s$ are i.i.d.\ with mean $g_s(\theta)$ and variance $\sigma_s^2(\theta)=\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid s)\le1$. We write $J(\theta)\in\mathbb{R}^{S\times p}$ for the Jacobian $J_{sa}=\partial g_s/\partial\theta_a$, evaluated at $\theta^\star$ unless noted. This is a standard smooth parametric model: the estimand is the residual, and \eqref{eq:born-mean} is the only channel through which data see it.

\paragraph{Identifiability from a Fisher-information rank condition.} The coefficients are recoverable exactly when the design's sensitivity matrix has full column rank. Theorem~\ref{thm:identifiability} shows that a design of Pauli-basis probe--readout pairs makes the Jacobian $J(\theta^\star)$ full rank at leading order in $\dt$, so all $p$ truncated coefficients are locally identifiable and the Fisher information $\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)$ is nonsingular. Identifiability is a rank condition, not an accident of the design: it holds precisely because the weight-$\leq w$ Pauli strings are linearly independent traceless observables, the same orthogonality that made the Frobenius projection of Theorem~\ref{thm:pauli-projection} well posed.

\paragraph{A Cram\'er--Rao floor, and its near-attainment.} Once the model is identifiable, information geometry fixes the best achievable variance. Theorem~\ref{thm:crb} gives the Cram\'er--Rao lower bound $\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\theta)\succeq\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)^{-1}=\tfrac1N\,\bigl(J^\top\Sigma^{-1}J\,\Xi\bigr)^{-1}$ (with $\Sigma=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_s^2)$ and $\Xi=\operatorname{diag}(\pi_s)$) for \emph{any} unbiased estimator of the residual coefficients, hence a matching lower bound $r\dt^{q+1}\!\sqrt{\operatorname{tr}\mathcal{I}^{-1}}$ on the standard deviation of the resulting global simulation-error certificate. This is the quantitative meaning of ``certified vs.\ estimated'': the deterministic truncation certificate of Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth} is what one recovers in the $N\to\infty$ limit of this bound, and the CRB says no estimator can do better at finite $N$. The maximum-likelihood (or, for the Gaussian shot-noise limit, generalized-least-squares) estimator attains this floor asymptotically (Theorem~\ref{thm:mle}): it is consistent, asymptotically normal with covariance $\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)^{-1}$, and therefore efficient, so the natural estimator of the residual is (asymptotically) the best possible one.

\paragraph{A finite-sample certified truncation.} The main inference result unifies the two error sources. Theorem~\ref{thm:certified-truncation} shows that the estimated, level-$w$ truncated residual $\widehat\Pi_wK_q$ obeys, with probability at least $1-\delta$,
\begin{equation}
    \norm{K_q-\widehat\Pi_wK_q}_2\ \le\ \underbrace{\tau_w}_{\text{truncation bias}}\ +\ \underbrace{\sqrt{\tfrac{2p}{N}\,\log\tfrac{2p}{\delta}}\;\kappa_w}_{\text{statistical error}},
    \label{eq:certified-truncation-intro}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_w=\norm{K_q-\Pi_wK_q}_2$ is the deterministic tail of Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth} (geometrically small in $w$ by Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate}) and $\kappa_w$ is an explicit design-conditioning constant. Composed with the multi-step stability bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:residual-stability}), this yields a global $(\epsilon,\delta)$ certificate: the learned truncated correction is within $\epsilon$ of exact time evolution with confidence $1-\delta$, provided $w$ is chosen to make the bias $r\tau_w\le\epsilon/2$ (the a priori choice $w^\star$ of Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}) and $N=\tilde O(p\,\kappa_w^2\,r^2/\epsilon^2)$ shots are spent to make the statistical term $\le\epsilon/2$. The bias half is deterministic and free of sampling; only the second half carries a confidence level, and it is confined to the coefficients that structure could not fix in closed form---exactly the delta-learning target of Sec.~\ref{sec:learned}. All four results are proved in Appendix~\ref{app:inference}; they add an inference layer to the framework without altering any reported number, since every experiment in this manuscript is computed or certified rather than sampled.

The per-step resource proxy separating baseline product-formula factors from dense oracle residual construction is given in Table~\ref{tab:resources}. The residual matrix is not counted as a quantum gate; any practical implementation must replace dense residual construction by a compiled circuit, a local commutator formula, a learned generator, or another efficient representation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The exact residual identity alone is not a practical algorithm. Its value is as the certified target of a chain of constructions that turn it into one, and the framework combines: (i) a uniquely defined optimal correction target; (ii) stability theorems---shown order-optimal---that translate residual approximation error into global simulation error; (iii) a generator view exposing the defect as a small Hermitian operator; (iv) a spectral-truncation projection (Definition~\ref{def:spectral-truncation}) that makes compressed generators mathematically well posed with an \emph{a priori} certificate at every truncation level, and that converges \emph{geometrically} in the level (Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate}), so the bandwidth is selectable from the target accuracy alone (Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}); (v) an order-generality locality theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality}) showing the defect stays low-weight, hence compressible, at every order; (vi) a faithful-compilation theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation}) that converts the compressed generator into a counted two-qubit-gate circuit with a controlled error budget, and a symmetric refinement (Theorem~\ref{thm:symmetric-compilation}) that removes the low-order compilation floor; (vii) the resulting error--cost frontier advance (Sec.~\ref{sec:frontier}); and (viii) a demonstration (Sec.~\ref{sec:learned}) that the generator can be \emph{learned} as a geometrically local, size-transferable operator inheriting the stability guarantee. This is a complete instance of ``oracle-to-algorithm'' research: many quantum algorithms begin with an ideal mathematical object and then build efficient block encodings, signal-processing polynomials, compilation methods, or randomized estimators around it~\cite{berry2015prl,low2017qsp,low2019qubitization,childs2022}. Here the ideal object is the residual generator, and the contribution is to compress it, compile it, learn it, and show that doing so moves the algorithm's accuracy--cost frontier.

Seen through the statistical lens of the introduction, the contribution is a case study in when estimation can be avoided. The naive way to obtain a finite-step correction on hardware is to characterize it---drive the residual channel and reconstruct it by process tomography, paying a sample complexity that grows with the dimension and returning an estimate with a confidence interval. We instead exploit enough structure---unitarity, locality, and low Pauli weight at every order---that the correction never has to be sampled. Where it is computed in software it is exact; where it must be predicted, the spectral-truncation certificate of Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth} supplies a deterministic upper bound on the resulting simulation error, $\norm{\widehat G_{q,w}^{\,r}-U(t)}_2\leq r\norm{K_q-\Pi_wK_q}_2$, that plays the role a confidence interval plays for an estimator: it bounds the error before the dynamics are run, and it is read off the generator rather than from repeated shots. The one place genuine estimation survives---the learned per-site generator of Sec.~\ref{sec:learned}---is exactly where structure stops short of a closed form, and even there the estimate transfers across size and inherits the same a priori bound. This is the concrete sense in which the work belongs to the statistics-of-quantum-computing programme of the introduction: not by injecting sampling noise into a deterministic algorithm, but by marking where the right structure lets one trade an estimate for a guarantee.

\rgtc{} differs from existing strategies but is compatible with them. Multi-product formulas cancel leading Trotter errors by linearly combining unitaries~\cite{childs2012mpf}; randomized formulas reduce error in expectation or concentration~\cite{campbell2019,ouyang2020,childs2022,chen2022a}; qubitization and quantum signal processing implement polynomial transformations of block encodings~\cite{berry2007,berry2014,low2017qsp,low2019qubitization}; Magnus methods construct effective generators for time-dependent or interaction-picture dynamics~\cite{magnus1954,blanes2009,sharma2024,fang2025}. \rgtc{} keeps the product-formula step and corrects it by a residual generator, so one could define a residual for a randomized product-formula realization, a multi-product formula, or a low-depth Cartan-decomposition circuit~\cite{kokcu2022,steckmann2023}.

The unifying conceptual import of the work is the \emph{spectral truncation} of the residual generator, which we position deliberately against the operator-algebraic spectral truncations recently developed in kernel learning~\cite{hashimoto2024spectral,hashimoto2023rkhm}. Both contract a noncommutative algebra to a tractable, structure-respecting subspace through a single interpolation parameter, and in both the parameter trades expressivity against tractability. The simulation setting, however, supplies a stronger object than the learning setting: the truncated quantity governs a concrete dynamical error, so the truncation acquires (i) a quantitative geometric convergence rate, not merely convergence to the untruncated limit (Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate}); (ii) an a priori dynamical certificate that selects the truncation level from the target accuracy (Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}), in place of a purely structural guarantee; and (iii) a gate-level realization whose own structural defect---the first-order inner-compilation floor---is repaired by a symmetric construction (Theorem~\ref{thm:symmetric-compilation}) that mirrors the positivity-restoring smoothing of the kernel construction. We therefore regard the contribution not as an analogy borrowed from operator-algebraic learning but as the strengthening that becomes available once spectral truncation is applied to an object that controls a dynamics: a rate, a certificate, and a measurable cost--accuracy payoff.

The projected residual experiment is the most important empirical component beyond the exact oracle identity. In the dense \tfim{} benchmark at $n=5$, $q=2$, $t=1$, the baseline error is $1.384\times10^{-2}$; the $w\leq3$ projected generator reduces this to $1.545\times10^{-4}$, while $w\leq4$ reduces it to $1.850\times10^{-7}$. These are still dense offline experiments, but they demonstrate that most of the useful correction can live in a dramatically smaller operator family, and Theorem~\ref{thm:tfim-support} explains why weight three is the first meaningful threshold for the leading Strang defect in this regime.

Near-term quantum algorithms, including VQE~\cite{peruzzo2014,mcclean2016}, QAOA~\cite{farhi2014,huynh2026gctr}, and broader variational workflows~\cite{cerezo2021,bharti2022,endo2021}, are constrained by circuit depth and noise~\cite{preskill2018,kandala2017,kim2023}. A shallow residual generator could be used as a correction layer after a product-formula block, but only if it can be compiled into native gates with tolerable overhead. Fault-tolerant settings shift the question toward T-count, block encodings, and resource estimates~\cite{wecker2014,wecker2015,babbush2018,babbush2019,haah2021}. In either regime, Theorem~\ref{thm:residual-stability} supplies a simple target: if a compiled residual has per-step operator error at most $\epsilon/r$, then the global simulation error contribution is at most $\epsilon$. Crucially, the correction is produced \emph{offline} and at a cost independent of the simulated system: the learned generator is fit from fixed-size local patches with no dense $2^n$ propagator, and sparse Pauli arithmetic reconstructs and scores generators to $n=10$ ($1024$-dimensional) without ever materializing dense Pauli matrices, so the classical work of obtaining the correction is amortized and size-independent. The benchmark suite is dense-linear-algebra and parameter-sweep heavy, hence naturally GPU-accelerated, which would enlarge the offline training sets and compression families benchmarked without changing the theory.

Four limitations bound the frontier claim, and we state each precisely. \emph{First, the cost advantage is not uniform.} The correction's two-qubit-gate count grows slightly faster with $n$ than the next standard order, so the corrected fourth-order step is more accurate than sixth order at comparable cost but is not cheaper than sixth order. The robust statement (Sec.~\ref{sec:frontier}) is that it reaches a new accuracy regime---between consecutive standard orders---at $4.2$--$5.2\times$ fewer two-qubit gates than the cheapest standard formula attaining the same accuracy. \emph{Second, the first-order inner compilation has a structural accuracy floor} (Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation}) that we have now removed rather than merely flagged: the symmetric inner compilation of Theorem~\ref{thm:symmetric-compilation} lowers the inner error to $O(\dt^{3(q+1)})$ and recovers the truncated-oracle accuracy at every base order---e.g.\ $2.18\times10^{-5}\!\to\!3.76\times10^{-7}$ at $n=6$, $q=2$, $w\le4$ (Table~\ref{tab:faithful-compilation})---at roughly twice the correction's two-qubit-gate count. What remains open is the cost--accuracy trade of this refinement on the frontier: because the symmetric compile doubles the correction layers, the headline $q=4$ frontier advance still uses the cheaper, already-faithful first-order compile, and a gate-optimal symmetric compile (e.g.\ exploiting partial layer commutation to avoid full doubling) is left to future work. \emph{Third, the oracle-free realization of the fourth-order frontier correction is only partly established.} On the positive side, that correction is built entirely from local weight-$\leq5$ templates and stays Pareto-optimal to $n=10$ (Table~\ref{tab:oracle-free-q4}), so by Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality} it is in principle obtainable from a fixed, size-independent reference patch. But we verified that a small ($8$-qubit) patch is not yet bulk-converged for the $q=4$ generator---its tiled coefficients reach only $\sim10^{-7}$ at $n=10$, an order of magnitude above the direct local correction---so a fully oracle-free realization at frontier accuracy requires a larger size-independent patch or the learned per-site map demonstrated at second order (transferring to ten qubits). Verifying either at frontier accuracy needs a ${>}12$-qubit patch with a still-larger dense target, beyond our dense reach ($n\le10$); we report the transfer gap rather than claim full convergence. \emph{Fourth}, evaluation is by dense simulation up to ten qubits and the templates are model-specific; the XXZ results (Sec.~\ref{sec:xxz}) show the mechanism generalizes but with a larger, splitting-dependent weight threshold. The paper proves correction, compression, and compilation guarantees and exhibits a concrete frontier advance on benchmark spin chains; it does not claim a universal asymptotic quantum speedup. These limitations define the next tasks---gate-optimal symmetric compilation, learned fourth-order corrections at scale, evaluation beyond dense simulation, and template families for broader Hamiltonians---each of which is implementation within the established principle rather than a change of it.

\section{Conclusion and outlook}

We have shown that the finite-step error of a product formula---usually the rare deterministic exception in a discipline where almost everything is estimated under sampling noise---is better understood as the same kind of inference object handled well: a structured operator to be compressed, certified, compiled, and learned rather than sampled. Recast as a Lie-algebraic spectral truncation of the residual generator, the correction inherits a geometric convergence rate and an a priori accuracy certificate and---applied to an already-high-order step---advances the product-formula error--cost frontier at a fraction of the next order's two-qubit-gate cost. Because the residual generator is geometrically local at every order, it is learnable offline from fixed-size local patches with no dense $2^n$ oracle and transfers across system size, so the correction is obtainable at scale rather than only in the dense-simulable regime. The immediate next steps are concrete: compile and certify the learned correction on hardware, extend the local templates to higher orders and broader Hamiltonians, and exploit GPU-accelerated offline fitting to push the compression beyond dense simulation. More broadly, the result reframes the truncation error of a numerical integrator as a learnable, geometrically local, transferable operator with \emph{a priori} error control, and it turns the claim that statistics is structural to quantum computing into a constructive one: the task is not only to estimate well under sampling noise but to recognize where structure lets an estimate be replaced by a certificate---a template we expect to apply beyond Hamiltonian simulation.

\section{Trotter--Suzuki construction and the residual factor}
\label{app:methods}

For $k\geq2$, higher even orders are generated by the Suzuki recursion
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
    S_{2k}(\dt)=S_{2k-2}(p_k\dt)^2S_{2k-2}((1-4p_k)\dt)S_{2k-2}(p_k\dt)^2,
    \qquad
    p_k=\left(4-4^{1/(2k-1)}\right)^{-1}.
    \label{eq:suzuki-recursion}
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
For a fixed order $q$ and step size $\dt$, the residual factor is $R_q(\dt)=U(\dt)S_q(\dt)^\dagger$, the corrected one-step propagator is $G_q(\dt)=R_q(\dt)S_q(\dt)$, and the global approximation is $\widetilde U_{R,q}(t;r)=G_q(t/r)^r$. When $R_q$ is computed exactly, $G_q(\dt)=U(\dt)$. Because $R_q$ is unitary, it admits the representation $R_q(\dt)=e^{-iK_q(\dt)}$ with a Hermitian residual generator $K_q$. The generator is not unique globally because eigenphases are defined modulo $2\pi$; for small enough $\dt$ the residual lies close to the identity and the principal generator is well defined.

A compressed residual generator is $\widehat K_q(\dt)\in\cB$, where $\cB\subseteq\mathsf{Herm}(d)$ is a real linear space of implementable Hermitian generators (Pauli strings of weight at most $w$, geometrically local Pauli strings of bounded diameter, commutator monomials up to a fixed degree, or generators representable by a fixed hardware ansatz), with $\widehat R_q(\dt)=e^{-i\widehat K_q(\dt)}$ and $\widehat G_q(\dt)=\widehat R_q(\dt)S_q(\dt)$. The most direct dense benchmark instantiation is Pauli projection. With $\cP_n=\{I,X,Y,Z\}^{\otimes n}$ and $\cB_w=\Span_\mathbb{R}\{P\in\cP_n:\operatorname{wt}(P)\leq w\}$, the projection is
\begin{equation}
    \Pi_wK_q=\sum_{\operatorname{wt}(P)\leq w}\frac{\Tr(PK_q)}{2^n}P.
    \label{eq:pauli-proj}
\end{equation}

\section{Numerical methodology and cost model}
\label{app:numerics}

All numerical results are generated by deterministic dense-matrix scripts that build the Pauli matrices, construct $A$, $B$, and $H$, evaluate matrix exponentials using dense linear algebra, compute spectral norms from singular values, and write the resulting CSV, JSON, PDF, and table files. The plotting pipeline contains no hand-coded performance numbers, and the deterministic studies use fixed grids with no stochastic sampling. The learned-residual experiment (Sec.~\ref{sec:learned}) does draw disordered couplings and train a network, but it is made reproducible by fixed random seeds for the sampler, the data split, and the network initialization; its training labels and all reported error metrics are computed by the same dense-matrix code, so rerunning the script reproduces every reported value. The benchmark design deliberately includes checks that would expose fabrication or implementation errors: unitarity checks for $S_q$, $R_q$, and corrected steps; same-order comparisons between $S_q$ and $R_qS_q$; exact dense recomputation of $U(t)$ rather than relying on repeated one-step products as the reference; saved raw data for every plotted point; and finite-precision reporting, including cases where round-off makes the high-order baseline comparable to the oracle residual.

The Hilbert-space dimension is $d=2^n$. For the fixed-time study we use $J=h=1$, $t=1$, and $r=10$ steps. The exact total propagator $U(t)$ is computed as $\exp(-iHt)$ independently of the product-formula steps. For the projected residual study we compute the residual generator $K_q=i\log(R_q)$ using the dense matrix logarithm, symmetrize $K_q$ as $(K_q+K_q^\dagger)/2$ to remove round-off skew-Hermitian contamination, and project onto Pauli strings of weight at most $w$ using Eq.~\eqref{eq:pauli-proj}. The projected corrected propagator is $[e^{-i\Pi_wK_q}S_q]^r$. The learned-residual study additionally trains small multilayer perceptrons (on global-oracle and on oracle-free local-patch labels) with a standard automatic-differentiation library on disordered chains; all couplings, step sizes, and network weights are seeded from a single fixed seed, and every learned generator is scored only through the dense spectral-norm metrics above. Each network is trained for $4000$ full-batch Adam steps on $220$ disordered realizations at each of $n=4$ and $n=5$; reported transfer errors are averaged over independent held-out realizations at each size ($40$ realizations for $n=4$--$7$, $24$ for $n=8$, $16$ for $n=9$, $12$ for $n=10$), and the shaded bands in Fig.~\ref{fig:learned} report one standard deviation across these realizations. All plotted errors are spectral-norm errors. Where a plotted oracle residual appears around $10^{-14}$ to $10^{-13}$, that value is the observed double-precision floor, not a mathematical error in exact arithmetic.

\paragraph{Two-qubit-gate cost model.} The error--cost frontier (Sec.~\ref{sec:frontier}, Fig.~\ref{fig:frontier}, Table~\ref{tab:frontier}) uses the standard textbook compilation of Pauli exponentials into Clifford-plus-rotation gates, accounting only two-qubit (CNOT) gates as is conventional in fault-tolerant and near-term resource estimates. For the \tfim{}, $e^{-iB\tau}$ is a layer of single-qubit $X$ rotations (no two-qubit gates), and $e^{-iA\tau}$ is a layer of $n-1$ nearest-neighbour $ZZ$ rotations, each costing two CNOTs, so an $A$-layer costs $2(n-1)$ CNOTs; a Suzuki step $S_q$ with $\#A_q$ elementary $A$-exponentials costs $2(n-1)\,\#A_q$ CNOTs, with $\#A_q=1,5,25,125$ for $q=2,4,6,8$. A weight-$w$ Pauli rotation $e^{-i\theta P}$ compiles by a CNOT ladder into $2(w-1)$ CNOTs. The compiled correction is partitioned into mutually-commuting layers by greedy colouring of the anticommutation graph (Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation}), and \emph{every reported corrected-step error is the error of this compiled correction}, not of the dense oracle; its CNOT cost is the sum of $2(w-1)$ over the retained Pauli terms. For the symmetric inner compilation (Theorem~\ref{thm:symmetric-compilation}, Table~\ref{tab:faithful-compilation}) each commuting layer is applied twice at half angle with the two central half-layers merged, so the correction cost rises to $2\sum_i\mathrm{cost}(L_i)-\mathrm{cost}(L_m)$ over the $m$ layers; we report both costs. Single-qubit gates are not counted; the classical cost of obtaining the generator is offline and, in the learned mode (Sec.~\ref{sec:learned}), amortized and free of any dense $2^n$ propagator.

\section{Proofs of the main results}
\label{app:proofs}

This section contains the main mathematical guarantees. All norms are spectral norms unless explicitly stated otherwise.

\begin{lemma}[Unitarity of product-formula steps]
\label{lem:Sq-unitary}
Let $A$ and $B$ be Hermitian and let every Suzuki coefficient be real. Then $S_q(\dt)$ is unitary for every real $\dt$ and every $q\in\{1,2,4,6,8\}$ generated by the Strang step and Eq.~\eqref{eq:suzuki-recursion}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Each elementary factor has the form $e^{-iC\tau}$ with $C=C^\dagger$ and $\tau\in\mathbb{R}$. Such a factor is unitary because $(e^{-iC\tau})^\dagger=e^{iC\tau}=(e^{-iC\tau})^{-1}$. The product of finitely many unitary matrices is unitary. The recursive Suzuki construction only composes such factors with real rescaled times, including the negative coefficient $1-4p_k$, which still gives a unitary exponential.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Exact residual factorization and uniqueness]
\label{thm:factorization}
Let $H=H^\dagger$ and let $S_q(\dt)$ be any unitary product-formula step. The residual $R_q(\dt)=U(\dt)S_q(\dt)^\dagger$ is unitary and is the unique matrix $L$ satisfying $LS_q(\dt)=U(\dt)$. Consequently, $R_q(\dt)S_q(\dt)=U(\dt)$ and $[R_q(t/r)S_q(t/r)]^r=U(t)$ for time-independent $H$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $H$ is Hermitian, $U(\dt)$ is unitary. Lemma~\ref{lem:Sq-unitary} gives unitarity of $S_q$, hence $S_q^\dagger=S_q^{-1}$. Thus $R_q^\dagger R_q=S_qU^\dagger US_q^\dagger=S_qS_q^\dagger=\id$. Moreover, $R_qS_q=U S_q^\dagger S_q=U$. If $L S_q=U$, right multiplication by $S_q^\dagger$ gives $L=US_q^\dagger=R_q$. For $t=r\dt$, time independence gives $U(\dt)^r=U(r\dt)=U(t)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Optimality among all left corrections]
\label{thm:optimality}
Let $|||\cdot|||$ be any unitarily invariant norm. The residual $R_q$ is a global minimizer of $\mathcal{J}(L)=|||U(\dt)-LS_q(\dt)|||$. The minimum value is zero, and the minimizer is unique.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Every norm is nonnegative, so $\mathcal{J}(L)\geq0$. Theorem~\ref{thm:factorization} gives $\mathcal{J}(R_q)=0$. If another $L'$ also gives zero, then $L'S_q=U$, and uniqueness in Theorem~\ref{thm:factorization} implies $L'=R_q$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:optimality} is an oracle statement, not a cost statement. It says that no exact left multiplier can beat $R_q$ in error because the error is already zero. It does not say that $R_q$ is cheap to implement.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Small-step residual-generator bound]
\label{thm:small-step}
Assume $S_q$ is order $q$ in the sense that there exist constants $C_q$ and $\dt_0$ such that $\norm{U(\dt)-S_q(\dt)}_2\leq C_q\abs{\dt}^{q+1}$ for $\abs{\dt}\leq\dt_0$. If $C_q\abs{\dt}^{q+1}\leq1$, then $R_q(\dt)$ has a Hermitian logarithm $K_q(\dt)$ with phases chosen in $[-\pi,\pi]$ and $\norm{K_q(\dt)}_2\leq 2C_q\abs{\dt}^{q+1}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Because $S_q$ is unitary, $\norm{R_q-\id}_2=\norm{US_q^\dagger-\id}_2=\norm{U-S_q}_2\leq C_q\abs{\dt}^{q+1}$. Diagonalize $R_q=W\operatorname{diag}(e^{-i\theta_j})W^\dagger$ with $\theta_j\in[-\pi,\pi]$ and define $K_q=W\operatorname{diag}(\theta_j)W^\dagger$. For any eigenphase, $\abs{e^{-i\theta_j}-1}=2\abs{\sin(\theta_j/2)}$. If $\norm{R_q-\id}_2\leq1$, then $\abs{\theta_j}\leq2\arcsin(\norm{R_q-\id}_2/2)\leq2\norm{R_q-\id}_2$. Taking the maximum over $j$ proves the bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Multi-step stability for approximate residuals]
\label{thm:residual-stability}
Let $\widehat R_q$ be any approximate residual and define $\widehat G_q=\widehat R_qS_q$. If $\eta=\norm{\widehat R_q-R_q}_2$, then
\begin{equation}
    \norm{\widehat G_q^{\;r}-U(t)}_2
    \leq \eta\sum_{j=0}^{r-1}(1+\eta)^j
    \leq r\eta e^{(r-1)\eta}.
    \label{eq:nonunitary-stability}
\end{equation}
If $\widehat R_q$ is unitary, then the sharper bound $\norm{\widehat G_q^{\;r}-U(t)}_2\leq r\eta$ holds.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $S_q$ is unitary, $\norm{\widehat G_q-G_q}_2=\norm{(\widehat R_q-R_q)S_q}_2=\eta$. Also $G_q=U(\dt)$ and $\norm{G_q}_2=1$. The telescoping identity $\widehat G_q^{\;r}-G_q^r=\sum_{j=0}^{r-1}\widehat G_q^{\;r-1-j}(\widehat G_q-G_q)G_q^j$ combined with submultiplicativity gives Eq.~\eqref{eq:nonunitary-stability}, because $\norm{\widehat G_q}_2\leq\norm{G_q}_2+\eta=1+\eta$. If $\widehat R_q$ is unitary, then $\widehat G_q$ is unitary and every factor in the telescoping sum has norm one except $\widehat G_q-G_q$, giving the sharper bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Tightness of the multi-step stability bound]
\label{thm:stability-tight}
Let $\dt>0$, let $H=H^\dagger$, and let $S_q(\dt)$ be an order-$q$ step with exact residual $R_q$ and corrected step $G_q=R_qS_q=U(\dt)$. For every $\eta\in(0,\pi]$ there exists a \emph{unitary} approximate residual $\widehat R_q$ with $\norm{\widehat R_q-R_q}_2\leq\eta$ such that, with $\widehat G_q=\widehat R_qS_q$,
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
    \norm{\widehat G_q^{\;r}-U(r\dt)}_2\geq\tfrac{2}{\pi}\,r\eta
    \qquad\text{for every integer } r \text{ with } r\eta\leq\pi.
    \label{eq:stability-tight}
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
Hence the linear-in-$r$ estimate of Theorem~\ref{thm:residual-stability} is order-optimal: no bound of the form $o(r\eta)$ can hold uniformly in $r$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $P=P^\dagger$ be a spectral projector of $H$ onto a single eigenspace, so $\norm{P}_2=1$ and $[P,U(s)]=0$ for all real $s$. Set $\widehat R_q=e^{-i\eta P}R_q$, which is unitary. Since $R_q$ is unitary, $\norm{\widehat R_q-R_q}_2=\norm{e^{-i\eta P}-\id}_2=\max_{p\in\{0,1\}}\abs{e^{-i\eta p}-1}=2\sin(\eta/2)\leq\eta$. Because $R_qS_q=U(\dt)$ and $[P,U(\dt)]=0$,
\begin{equation*}
    \widehat G_q^{\;r}=\bigl(e^{-i\eta P}U(\dt)\bigr)^r=e^{-ir\eta P}U(\dt)^r=e^{-ir\eta P}U(r\dt).
\end{equation*}
Therefore $\norm{\widehat G_q^{\;r}-U(r\dt)}_2=\norm{e^{-ir\eta P}-\id}_2=2\sin(r\eta/2)$. For $r\eta\leq\pi$, Jordan's inequality $\sin x\geq\frac{2}{\pi}x$ on $[0,\pi/2]$ gives $2\sin(r\eta/2)\geq\frac{2}{\pi}r\eta$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Duhamel bound for generator approximation]
\label{thm:duhamel}
Let $K$ and $\widehat K$ be Hermitian. Then $\norm{e^{-iK}-e^{-i\widehat K}}_2\leq \norm{K-\widehat K}_2$. Consequently, if $R_q=e^{-iK_q}$ and $\widehat R_q=e^{-i\widehat K_q}$, then $\norm{\widehat G_q^{\;r}-U(t)}_2\leq r\norm{K_q-\widehat K_q}_2$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Define $F(s)=e^{-i[(1-s)K+s\widehat K]}$. The derivative is represented by the standard Duhamel formula $\frac{dF}{ds}=-i\int_0^1 e^{-i(1-a)H_s}(\widehat K-K)e^{-iaH_s}\,da$, where $H_s=(1-s)K+s\widehat K$. Since $H_s$ is Hermitian, the exponentials in the integral are unitary, so $\norm{dF/ds}_2\leq\norm{\widehat K-K}_2$. Integrating from $s=0$ to $s=1$ proves the first bound. The global bound follows from Theorem~\ref{thm:residual-stability}, since both exponentials are unitary.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Lie-algebraic spectral truncation]
\label{def:spectral-truncation}
The Pauli-weight subspaces $\cB_0\subseteq\cB_1\subseteq\cdots\subseteq\cB_n=\mathsf{Herm}(d)$ form a filtration of the Lie algebra of Hermitian operators by interaction range. We call the orthogonal projection $\Pi_w$ onto $\cB_w$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:pauli-proj}) the \emph{spectral truncation at level $w$}, and $\widehat G_{q,w}=e^{-i\Pi_wK_q}S_q$ the level-$w$ truncated residual compilation. The level $w$ is a single tunable knob interpolating from the uncorrected step ($w=0$) to the exact correction ($w=n$); Theorem~\ref{thm:pauli-projection} and Corollary~\ref{cor:projected-certificate} show every level carries an \emph{a priori} simulation certificate.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
This is the Hamiltonian-simulation counterpart of spectral-truncation constructions in operator-algebraic machine learning, where truncating a noncommutative algebra yields a tunable family interpolating between local and global models with provable approximation control~\cite{hashimoto2024spectral,hashimoto2023rkhm}. Here the algebra is the Lie algebra of generators, the truncation knob is interaction range, and the approximation control is the multi-step simulation certificate rather than a kernel-approximation bound. Two consequences distinguish the simulation setting: the truncation converges \emph{geometrically} in the level (Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate}), and the certificate is a bound on a downstream dynamical quantity, so the level is selectable from the target accuracy alone (Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}).
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Frobenius-optimal Pauli compression]
\label{thm:pauli-projection}
Let $K=K^\dagger$ on $n$ qubits and let $\cB\subseteq\Span_\mathbb{R}(\cP_n)$ be any real Pauli subspace. The orthogonal projection $\Pi_\cB K=\sum_{P\in\cB}\frac{\Tr(PK)}{2^n}P$ uniquely minimizes $\norm{K-L}_F$ over $L\in\cB$. The corresponding unitary residual approximation satisfies $\norm{e^{-iK}-e^{-i\Pi_\cB K}}_2\leq\norm{K-\Pi_\cB K}_2\leq\norm{K-\Pi_\cB K}_F$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The normalized Pauli operators $P/\sqrt{2^n}$ form an orthonormal basis under the Hilbert--Schmidt inner product. Orthogonal projection onto a closed finite-dimensional subspace is the unique Frobenius-norm minimizer. The spectral norm is bounded by the Frobenius norm, and Theorem~\ref{thm:duhamel} supplies the unitary perturbation bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Projected residual simulation certificate]
\label{cor:projected-certificate}
For $K_{q,w}=\Pi_wK_q$ and $\widehat G_{q,w}=e^{-iK_{q,w}}S_q$, $\norm{\widehat G_{q,w}^{\;r}-U(t)}_2\leq r\norm{K_q-K_{q,w}}_2$. Thus a target global accuracy $\epsilon$ is guaranteed whenever $\norm{K_q-K_{q,w}}_2\leq\epsilon/r$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Both $K_q$ and $K_{q,w}=\Pi_wK_q$ are Hermitian, the latter because $\Pi_w$ is a real-linear projection onto a span of Hermitian Pauli strings (Theorem~\ref{thm:pauli-projection}); hence $R_q=e^{-iK_q}$ and $\widehat R_{q,w}=e^{-iK_{q,w}}$ are both unitary. The Duhamel bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:duhamel}) gives the per-step estimate $\norm{\widehat R_{q,w}-R_q}_2=\norm{e^{-iK_{q,w}}-e^{-iK_q}}_2\leq\norm{K_q-K_{q,w}}_2=:\eta$. Since $\widehat R_{q,w}$ is unitary, the sharp branch of Theorem~\ref{thm:residual-stability} applies to $\widehat G_{q,w}=\widehat R_{q,w}S_q$ and yields $\norm{\widehat G_{q,w}^{\;r}-U(t)}_2\leq r\eta=r\norm{K_q-K_{q,w}}_2$. Imposing $\eta\leq\epsilon/r$ makes the right-hand side at most $\epsilon$, which is the stated sufficient condition.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Certificate-driven bandwidth selection]
\label{cor:bandwidth}
Fix a target global accuracy $\epsilon>0$, step count $r$, and step $\dt$, and let $\tau_w:=\norm{K_q-\Pi_wK_q}_2$ be the truncation tail at level $w$. The quantities $\tau_0\geq\tau_1\geq\cdots\geq\tau_n=0$ are nonincreasing and computable from $K_q$ alone (no time evolution). The bandwidth
\begin{equation}
    w^\star(\epsilon)=\min\{\,w:\ r\,\tau_w\leq\epsilon\,\}
\end{equation}
is well defined and the level-$w^\star$ truncated compilation meets the target, $\norm{\widehat G_{q,w^\star}^{\,r}-U(t)}_2\leq\epsilon$. If, moreover, $\tau_w\leq C\rho^{\,w}$ with $\rho<1$ (the geometric regime of Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate}), then $w^\star(\epsilon)\leq\log(rC/\epsilon)/\log(1/\rho)$ grows only logarithmically in $1/\epsilon$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Monotonicity of $\tau_w$ holds because $\cB_w\subseteq\cB_{w+1}$, so the orthogonal projection onto the larger subspace cannot increase the residual norm; $\tau_n=0$ because $\cB_n=\mathsf{Herm}(d)$ and $\Pi_nK_q=K_q$. Hence the set $\{w:r\tau_w\leq\epsilon\}$ contains $w=n$ and has a least element $w^\star$. The accuracy guarantee is Corollary~\ref{cor:projected-certificate} with $w=w^\star$, since $r\tau_{w^\star}\leq\epsilon$. For the logarithmic bound, $r\tau_w\leq rC\rho^{\,w}\leq\epsilon$ as soon as $w\geq\log(rC/\epsilon)/\log(1/\rho)$, and $w^\star$ is the least such integer.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Leading Strang residual support for the open-boundary TFIM]
\label{thm:tfim-support}
Let $A$ and $B$ be the \tfim{} splitting in Eq.~\eqref{eq:tfim}. For the Strang step $S_2(\dt)$, the degree-three homogeneous term in the residual generator $K_2(\dt)$ is a real linear combination of Pauli strings with weight at most three. Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
    K_2(\dt)=\dt^3K_2^{(3)}+O(\dt^5),
    \qquad
    K_2^{(3)}\in\cB_3.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The symmetric BCH expansion of Strang splitting has no degree-two term; the first nonzero defect is a linear combination of double commutators $[A,[A,B]]$ and $[B,[A,B]]$~\cite{magnus1954,blanes2009}. The residual generator is the negative of this leading logarithmic defect up to the same order, so it is enough to bound the Pauli weight of these double commutators.

Write $A=\sum_e A_e$ with $A_e=JZ_jZ_{j+1}$ for edges $e=(j,j+1)$ and $B=\sum_v B_v$ with $B_v=hX_v$. A commutator $[A_e,B_v]$ vanishes unless $v$ is incident to $e$. If it is nonzero, its support is contained in the two sites of $e$ and has Pauli weight two. In $[B,[A,B]]$, the second commutator adds at most one on-site term $B_{v'}$ whose support must overlap the existing support; therefore the support remains within the same edge and the Pauli weight is at most two. In $[A,[A,B]]$, the second edge term $A_{e'}$ must overlap the support of $[A_e,B_v]$. The union of two overlapping nearest-neighbor edges in a one-dimensional open chain contains at most three sites. Therefore every nonzero Pauli string in the double commutators has weight at most three. This proves $K_2^{(3)}\in\cB_3$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Order generality of residual locality]
\label{thm:order-locality}
Let $A=J\sum_e Z_jZ_{j+1}$ and $B=h\sum_v X_v$ be the open-boundary \tfim{} splitting, and let $S_q$ be a symmetric product formula of order $q$. The leading term of its residual generator is geometrically local of bounded Pauli weight:
\begin{equation}
    K_q(\dt)=\dt^{q+1}K_q^{(q+1)}+O(\dt^{q+3}),
    \qquad
    K_q^{(q+1)}\in\cB_{q+2}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The leading defect of a symmetric order-$q$ product formula is $O(\dt^{q+1})$, and by the symmetric Baker--Campbell--Hausdorff / Magnus structure its operator coefficient is a real linear combination of $(q{+}1)$-fold nested commutators of $A$ and $B$~\cite{magnus1954,blanes2009}; the residual generator cancels this leading logarithmic defect, so $K_q^{(q+1)}$ is itself such a linear combination. We bound the Pauli weight of any nonzero $m$-fold nested commutator of the \tfim{} local terms by induction on the number $m$ of local factors. A single factor $A_e=JZ_jZ_{j+1}$ or $B_v=hX_v$ has support of at most two sites, so weight $\le2=m+1$ at $m=1$. Assume an $(m{-}1)$-fold nested commutator $C$ has support on at most $m$ sites. A further commutator $[L,C]$ with a local factor $L$ of range at most two is nonzero only if $\supp(L)\cap\supp(C)\neq\emptyset$, and $\supp([L,C])\subseteq\supp(L)\cup\supp(C)$, a set of at most $\abs{\supp(C)}+2-1\le m+1$ sites. Hence every nonzero $m$-fold nested commutator has Pauli weight at most $m+1$, and the leading $(q{+}1)$-fold term has weight at most $q+2$, i.e.\ $K_q^{(q+1)}\in\cB_{q+2}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality} is the worst-case algebraic bound; in the symmetric formula many maximal-weight strings cancel, so the \emph{effective} compression threshold is lower. Table~\ref{tab:order-generality} measures the dominant collapse at weight $3,4,5$ for $q=2,4,6$, i.e.\ $\approx\lceil q/2\rceil+2$, comfortably inside the proven $q+2$ envelope and tight at $q=2$, where Theorem~\ref{thm:tfim-support} gives the sharper bound three.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Geometric convergence rate of the spectral truncation]
\label{thm:spectral-rate}
Let $A,B$ be the open-boundary \tfim{} splitting and $S_q$ the symmetric order-$q$ step. There exist constants $C_q>0$, a ratio $\rho\in(0,1)$ with $\rho=O(\dt)$, and $\dt_0>0$ such that, for $\dt\leq\dt_0$ and every integer $w$ with $q+1\leq w\leq n$, the level-$w$ spectral-truncation tail $\tau_w=\norm{K_q-\Pi_wK_q}_2$ obeys the \emph{geometric envelope}
\begin{equation}
    \tau_w\leq C_q\,\rho^{\,w}.
    \label{eq:spectral-rate}
\end{equation}
Consequently, by Corollary~\ref{cor:projected-certificate}, the certified global error $r\tau_w$ contracts by a factor $\rho=O(\dt)$ per added level, $\norm{\widehat G_{q,w}^{\,r}-U(t)}_2\leq r\,C_q\,\rho^{\,w}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The residual factor $R_q(\dt)=U(\dt)S_q(\dt)^\dagger$ is real-analytic in $\dt$ and equals $\id$ at $\dt=0$, so for $\abs{\dt}$ below some radius $\dt_0>0$ it remains in the domain on which the principal logarithm is analytic; hence $K_q(\dt)=i\log R_q(\dt)$ is real-analytic near $0$ and its homogeneous expansion $K_q(\dt)=\sum_{d\geq q+1}\dt^{\,d}K_q^{(d)}$ converges absolutely for $\abs{\dt}\leq\dt_0$, with $M:=\sum_{d\geq q+1}\dt_0^{\,d}\norm{K_q^{(d)}}_2<\infty$. (Time-reversal symmetry of $S_q$ makes only the degrees $q+1,q+3,\dots$ appear~\cite{blanes2009}, which we do not need beyond convergence.) By the nested-commutator induction of Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality}, $K_q^{(d)}\in\cB_{d+1}$, so any Pauli string of weight $\geq w+1$ first occurs at degree $d\geq w$; the part of $K_q$ outside $\cB_w$ is therefore a sum of homogeneous terms of degree $d\geq w$, and for $\dt\leq\dt_0$,
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation*}
    \tau_w=\Bigl\lVert\sum_{d\geq w}\dt^{\,d}\,\Pi_{>w}K_q^{(d)}\Bigr\rVert_2\leq\sum_{d\geq w}\dt^{\,d}\norm{K_q^{(d)}}_2=\Bigl(\tfrac{\dt}{\dt_0}\Bigr)^{\!w}\sum_{d\geq w}\Bigl(\tfrac{\dt}{\dt_0}\Bigr)^{\!d-w}\dt_0^{\,d}\norm{K_q^{(d)}}_2\leq M\,\Bigl(\tfrac{\dt}{\dt_0}\Bigr)^{\!w},
\end{equation*}
\end{widetext}
using $(\dt/\dt_0)^{d-w}\leq1$ for $d\geq w$. This is Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral-rate} with $C_q=M$ and $\rho=\dt/\dt_0=O(\dt)$. The global estimate follows from Corollary~\ref{cor:projected-certificate}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Equation~\eqref{eq:spectral-rate} is a conservative provable envelope (per-level contraction $\rho=O(\dt)$, i.e.\ a factor $\gtrsim\dt^{-1}=10$). The measured per-level factors are far larger---$449\times$ for $q=2$ (least-squares fit over $w=3,4,5$) and $576\times$ for $q=4$ (the single resolvable pair $w=4,5$ before the round-off floor) at $\dt=0.1$ (Table~\ref{tab:spectral-rate})---because the symmetric cancellations that lower the effective locality threshold to $\approx\lceil q/2\rceil+2$ (Remark after Theorem~\ref{thm:order-locality}) also raise the degree at which each new weight level first appears, so empirically each retained level absorbs content roughly two powers of $\dt$ smaller, i.e.\ an effective ratio $\approx\dt^{2}$ further amplified by prefactors. The provable envelope already gives a geometric \emph{rate}, not merely convergence in a limit, which is what makes the level a usable accuracy dial (Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}).
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Faithful gate-level compilation of the correction]
\label{thm:faithful-compilation}
Let the compressed generator be partitioned into $m$ layers $\widehat K_q=\sum_{i=1}^{m}L_i$, where each $L_i$ is a real linear combination of \emph{mutually commuting} Pauli strings, and let $\widehat R_q^{\mathrm{c}}=\prod_{i=1}^{m}e^{-iL_i}$ be the first-order compiled correction. Then, with $\Lambda=\sum_{i=1}^{m}\norm{L_i}_2$,
\begin{equation}
    \norm{\widehat R_q^{\mathrm{c}}-e^{-i\widehat K_q}}_2\le\tfrac12\sum_{i<j}\norm{[L_i,L_j]}_2\le\tfrac12\Lambda^2 .
\end{equation}
Consequently $\widehat G_q^{\mathrm{c}}=\widehat R_q^{\mathrm{c}}S_q$ obeys, for $t=r\dt$,
\begin{equation}
    \norm{(\widehat G_q^{\mathrm{c}})^{r}-U(t)}_2\le r\,\norm{K_q-\widehat K_q}_2+\tfrac12\,r\,\Lambda^2 .
\end{equation}
Since every coefficient of $\widehat K_q$ is $O(\dt^{q+1})$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:small-step}), $\Lambda=O(\dt^{q+1})$ and the compilation term is $O(r\,\dt^{2(q+1)})$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The first inequality is the standard first-order Lie--Trotter estimate for the difference between the ordered product $\prod_i e^{-iL_i}$ and $e^{-i\sum_i L_i}$, bounded to second order by $\tfrac12\sum_{i<j}\norm{[L_i,L_j]}_2$; using $\norm{[L_i,L_j]}_2\le2\norm{L_i}_2\norm{L_j}_2$ and $\sum_{i<j}2\norm{L_i}_2\norm{L_j}_2\le(\sum_i\norm{L_i}_2)^2=\Lambda^2$ gives the $\tfrac12\Lambda^2$ bound. The global estimate follows by the triangle inequality $\norm{\widehat R_q^{\mathrm{c}}-R_q}_2\le\norm{\widehat R_q^{\mathrm{c}}-e^{-i\widehat K_q}}_2+\norm{e^{-i\widehat K_q}-e^{-iK_q}}_2$, the Duhamel bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:duhamel}) on the second term, and the unitary multi-step bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:residual-stability}) applied with both $\widehat R_q^{\mathrm{c}}$ and $R_q$ unitary. Finally each $\norm{L_i}_2\le\sum_{P\in L_i}\abs{c_P}$ with $c_P$ the Pauli coefficients of $\widehat K_q$, so $\Lambda\le\sum_P\abs{c_P}=O(\dt^{q+1})$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation} is why the frontier advance is specific to $q\ge4$. The corrected step's intrinsic error after removing the leading defect is $O(\dt^{q+3})$, while the inner-compilation error is $O(\dt^{2(q+1)})$; the latter is asymptotically smaller exactly when $2(q+1)>q+3$, i.e.\ $q>1$, but the \emph{prefactors} matter at finite $\dt$: at $q=2$, $\dt=0.1$ the compilation term ($\sim\Lambda^2$ with $\Lambda\sim\norm{K_2}\sim10^{-3}$) caps the compiled $w\le4$ correction near $2\times10^{-5}$ (Table~\ref{tab:frontier}), whereas at $q=4$, $\Lambda\sim\norm{K_4}\sim10^{-6}$ and the compilation term is $\sim10^{-12}$, far below the corrected accuracy. The theory thus predicts, and the data confirm, that faithful compilation requires a sufficiently small base-step residual---i.e.\ a sufficiently high base order.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Symmetric faithful compilation]
\label{thm:symmetric-compilation}
With the layer partition $\widehat K_q=\sum_{i=1}^{m}L_i$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation}, let the \emph{symmetric} compiled correction be the palindromic product
\begin{equation}
    \widehat R_q^{\mathrm{s}}=\Bigl(\prod_{i=1}^{m}e^{-iL_i/2}\Bigr)\Bigl(\prod_{i=m}^{1}e^{-iL_i/2}\Bigr).
\end{equation}
Then, with $\Lambda=\sum_i\norm{L_i}_2$ and a constant $c$ depending only on $m$,
\begin{equation}
    \norm{\widehat R_q^{\mathrm{s}}-e^{-i\widehat K_q}}_2\leq c\,\Lambda^3 ,
\end{equation}
and consequently $\widehat G_q^{\mathrm{s}}=\widehat R_q^{\mathrm{s}}S_q$ obeys $\norm{(\widehat G_q^{\mathrm{s}})^{r}-U(t)}_2\leq r\norm{K_q-\widehat K_q}_2+c\,r\,\Lambda^3$. Since $\Lambda=O(\dt^{q+1})$, the inner-compilation term is $O(r\,\dt^{3(q+1)})$---one order of $(q+1)$ smaller than the first-order bound $O(r\,\dt^{2(q+1)})$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation}---at the cost of applying each layer twice, with the two central half-layers merging, so the correction's two-qubit-gate count rises from $\sum_i\mathrm{cost}(L_i)$ to $2\sum_i\mathrm{cost}(L_i)-\mathrm{cost}(L_m)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\widehat R_q^{\mathrm{s}}$ is the symmetric (Strang) composition of the one-parameter unitaries $e^{-isL_i}$, a time-symmetric second-order integrator for the flow of $-i\sum_iL_i=-i\widehat K_q$ over unit time. By the standard backward-error / symmetric-BCH analysis of such compositions~\cite{blanes2009}, the leading commutator defect of the first-order ordered product cancels by symmetry and the remaining error is third order in the generators, $\norm{\widehat R_q^{\mathrm{s}}-e^{-i\widehat K_q}}_2\leq c\sum_{i\leq j\leq k}\norm{[L_i,[L_j,L_k]]}_2\leq c'\Lambda^3$, absorbing $c'$ into $c$. The global estimate follows as in Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation}: triangle inequality through $e^{-i\widehat K_q}$, the Duhamel bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:duhamel}) on $\norm{e^{-i\widehat K_q}-e^{-iK_q}}_2$, and the unitary multi-step bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:residual-stability}). The gate count follows because each layer $L_i$ appears as two half-angle exponentials of identical CNOT cost to the full-angle one, except the central pair $e^{-iL_m/2}e^{-iL_m/2}=e^{-iL_m}$, which merges into a single layer.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:symmetric-compilation} removes the structural floor that Theorem~\ref{thm:faithful-compilation} leaves at low base order. At $n=6$, $q=2$, $w\le4$ the first-order compile stalls at $2.18\times10^{-5}$ (Table~\ref{tab:frontier}) against the dense-oracle $3.65\times10^{-7}$ (Table~\ref{tab:faithful-compilation}); the symmetric compile reaches $3.76\times10^{-7}$, recovering the oracle, at roughly twice the correction's CNOT count (Table~\ref{tab:faithful-compilation}). This is the simulation counterpart of the smoothing that restores positive-definiteness in operator-algebraic spectral truncation~\cite{hashimoto2024spectral}: a symmetry-motivated modification of the naive realization that repairs the truncated object's structural defect. At $q=4$ the first-order compile is already faithful and cheaper, so the headline frontier advance (Sec.~\ref{sec:frontier}) does not need the symmetric compile; its role is to make the compress-then-compile pipeline faithful at \emph{every} base order.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Size-transfer error bound for learned local residual generators]
\label{thm:size-transfer}
Consider the disordered open-boundary \tfim{} on $n$ sites with couplings in a fixed compact set, and let $K_2^{(3)}(n)=\sum_{j=1}^{n} h_j$ be the degree-three Strang residual generator, where by Theorem~\ref{thm:tfim-support} each $h_j$ is a real linear combination of weight-$\le 3$ Pauli strings supported in the three-site window $\{j-1,j,j+1\}$ (truncated at the boundaries). Suppose a translation-invariant per-site predictor outputs $\widehat h_j$ from the couplings in that window with $\norm{\widehat h_j-h_j}_2\le\eta$ for every $j$, where $\eta$ depends only on the predictor and the coupling range, not on $n$. Then $\widehat K_2^{(3)}(n)=\sum_j\widehat h_j$ satisfies $\norm{\widehat K_2^{(3)}(n)-K_2^{(3)}(n)}_2\le n\,\eta$, and the corrected step $\widehat G(\dt)=\exp(-i\dt^3\widehat K_2^{(3)})\,S_2(\dt)$ obeys, for $t=r\dt$, $\norm{\widehat G(\dt)^r-U(t)}_2\le r\,\dt^3 n\,\eta + r\,O(\dt^5)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The first bound is the triangle inequality over the $n$ site-anchored terms,
\begin{equation*}
    \norm{\textstyle\sum_j(\widehat h_j-h_j)}_2\le\sum_j\norm{\widehat h_j-h_j}_2\le n\eta;
\end{equation*}
translation invariance makes the per-site bound $\eta$ independent of~$n$. For the second, set $\widehat K=\dt^3\widehat K_2^{(3)}$. The BCH expansion in Appendix~\ref{app:bch} gives $K_2(\dt)=\dt^3K_2^{(3)}+O(\dt^5)$, so $\norm{\widehat K-K_2(\dt)}_2\le\dt^3 n\eta+O(\dt^5)$. The Duhamel bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:duhamel}) gives $\norm{e^{-i\widehat K}-R_2}_2\le\norm{\widehat K-K_2}_2$, and the multi-step stability bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:residual-stability} multiplies by~$r$, giving the stated estimate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[From per-site coefficient error to a global certificate]
\label{thm:coeff-certificate}
Adopt the setting of Theorem~\ref{thm:size-transfer} and expand each per-site generator in the fixed family of $T$ weight-$\leq3$ Pauli templates anchored at site $j$, $h_j=\sum_{\alpha=1}^{T}c_{j,\alpha}P_{j,\alpha}$ and $\widehat h_j=\sum_{\alpha=1}^{T}\widehat c_{j,\alpha}P_{j,\alpha}$, where each $P_{j,\alpha}$ is a Pauli string, so $\norm{P_{j,\alpha}}_2=1$. If the per-site coefficient error obeys $\norm{c_j-\widehat c_j}_2\leq\delta_c$ for every $j$, then the per-site operator error of Theorem~\ref{thm:size-transfer} satisfies $\eta\leq\sqrt{T}\,\delta_c$, and the learned corrected step $\widehat G(\dt)=\exp(-i\dt^3\widehat K_2^{(3)})\,S_2(\dt)$ obeys, for $t=r\dt$,
\begin{equation}
    \norm{\widehat G(\dt)^r-U(t)}_2\leq r\,\dt^3\,n\sqrt{T}\,\delta_c+r\,O(\dt^5).
    \label{eq:coeff-certificate}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the triangle inequality and $\norm{P_{j,\alpha}}_2=1$,
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation*}
    \norm{\widehat h_j-h_j}_2=\norm{\sum_{\alpha=1}^{T}(\widehat c_{j,\alpha}-c_{j,\alpha})P_{j,\alpha}}_2\leq\sum_{\alpha=1}^{T}\abs{\widehat c_{j,\alpha}-c_{j,\alpha}}=\norm{c_j-\widehat c_j}_1\leq\sqrt{T}\,\norm{c_j-\widehat c_j}_2\leq\sqrt{T}\,\delta_c,
\end{equation*}
\end{widetext}
where the last line uses the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality $\norm{x}_1\leq\sqrt{T}\,\norm{x}_2$ on $\mathbb{R}^T$. Taking the maximum over $j$ gives $\eta\leq\sqrt{T}\,\delta_c$; substituting this into the size-transfer bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:size-transfer} yields Eq.~\eqref{eq:coeff-certificate}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
With the $T=48$ contiguous weight-$\leq3$ templates per site used in Sec.~\ref{sec:learned} and the measured held-out coefficient accuracy (relative $\ell_2$ error $7.2\times10^{-3}$, $R^2=0.9999$), Theorem~\ref{thm:coeff-certificate} converts the learned-coefficient accuracy directly into the \emph{a priori} global error bound whose linear-in-$r$ growth Fig.~\ref{fig:learned}c verifies empirically.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:tfim-support} does not say the full residual generator lies in $\cB_3$. Higher-order BCH terms can produce larger support. It predicts that $w\leq3$ projection should remove the dominant Strang defect at small step size, while $w\leq4$ and higher should capture additional subleading structure. This is consistent with the numerical results reported above.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[A posteriori step certificate]
\label{thm:posteriori}
Let $\widehat G$ be any unitary approximate one-step propagator and let $\Delta=\norm{\widehat G-U(\dt)}_2$. Then $\norm{\widehat G^r-U(t)}_2\leq r\Delta$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Apply the telescoping identity to $\widehat G^r-U(\dt)^r$. Both $\widehat G$ and $U(\dt)$ are unitary, so each of the $r$ terms is bounded by $\Delta$.
\end{proof}

\section{Inference theory for the residual estimand}
\label{app:inference}

This appendix proves the four results of Sec.~\ref{sec:inference}, treating the Pauli coefficients of the (truncated) residual generator as the parameter of a smooth parametric statistical model and deriving its identifiability, information geometry, maximum-likelihood asymptotics, and a finite-sample certified truncation. All norms without a subscript are Euclidean on $\mathbb{R}^p$; for a symmetric matrix, $\succeq$ denotes the L\"owner (positive-semidefinite) order, and $\lambda_{\min}(\cdot)$, $\lambda_{\max}(\cdot)$ the extreme eigenvalues. We reuse the residual generator $K_q$, its Pauli expansion $c_P=\Tr(PK_q)/2^n$ (Appendix~\ref{app:projection}), the spectral truncation $\Pi_w$ (Definition~\ref{def:spectral-truncation}), and the tail $\tau_w=\norm{K_q-\Pi_wK_q}_2$ of Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}.

\subsection{The model and its regularity}
\label{app:inference-model}

We restate the measurement model of Sec.~\ref{sec:inference} in the form used by the proofs. Fix $q$, $\dt$, and level $w$, and let $\{P_1,\dots,P_p\}$ be a real orthogonal basis of the weight-$\leq w$ Pauli subspace $\cB_w$, normalized by the Hilbert--Schmidt product $\Tr(P_aP_b)=2^n\delta_{ab}$ (Appendix~\ref{app:projection}). Write $\Pi_wK_q=\sum_{a=1}^{p}\theta_aP_a$; the truncated residual is $R_q(\theta)=e^{-iK(\theta)}$ with $K(\theta)=\sum_a\theta_aP_a$, and the true value is $\theta^\star=(c_{P_1},\dots,c_{P_p})$. By Theorem~\ref{thm:small-step}, $\norm{K_q}_2=O(\dt^{q+1})$, so each $\abs{\theta_a^\star}=O(\dt^{q+1})$.

A design consists of settings $s=1,\dots,S$, each a probe state $\rho_s\succeq0$, $\Tr\rho_s=1$, and readout $M_s=M_s^\dagger$ with $\norm{M_s}_2\leq1$; setting $s$ is allocated a fixed fraction $\pi_s>0$ of $N$ i.i.d.\ shots, $N_s=\pi_sN$, $\sum_s\pi_s=1$. The Born-rule mean $g_s(\theta)$ is \eqref{eq:born-mean}, and we collect $g(\theta)=(g_1,\dots,g_S)^\top$, the Jacobian $J(\theta)\in\mathbb{R}^{S\times p}$ with $J_{sa}=\partial_{\theta_a}g_s(\theta)$, the noise matrix $\Sigma(\theta)=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1^2(\theta),\dots,\sigma_S^2(\theta))$ with $\sigma_s^2(\theta)=\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid s)$, and $\Xi=\operatorname{diag}(\pi_1,\dots,\pi_S)$.

\begin{lemma}[Smoothness and derivative of the Born mean]
\label{lem:born-smooth}
Each $g_s$ is real-analytic on $\mathbb{R}^p$ with $\abs{g_s(\theta)}\leq1$, and
\begin{equation}
    \partial_{\theta_a}g_s(\theta)\big|_{\theta=0}=\Tr\!\bigl(M_s\,i[\rho_s,P_a]\bigr),
    \qquad
    \sigma_s^2(\theta)\leq1 .
    \label{eq:born-derivative}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The map $\theta\mapsto R_q(\theta)=e^{-iK(\theta)}$ is entire (a matrix exponential of a linear function of $\theta$), and $g_s$ is a fixed polynomial (trace of a product) in the entries of $R_q(\theta)$ and $R_q(\theta)^\dagger$, hence real-analytic; $\abs{g_s}\leq\norm{M_s}_2\leq1$ since $R_q\rho_sR_q^\dagger$ is a density matrix. Differentiating \eqref{eq:born-mean},
\begin{equation*}
    \partial_{\theta_a}g_s=\Tr\!\bigl[M_s(\partial_{\theta_a}R_q)\rho_sR_q^\dagger+M_sR_q\rho_s(\partial_{\theta_a}R_q)^\dagger\bigr].
\end{equation*}
At $\theta=0$, $R_q=\id$ and $\partial_{\theta_a}R_q\big|_0=-iP_a$ (the derivative of $e^{-iK(\theta)}$ at $K=0$ is $-i\,\partial_{\theta_a}K=-iP_a$). Substituting gives $\partial_{\theta_a}g_s\big|_0=\Tr[M_s(-iP_a\rho_s+i\rho_sP_a)]=\Tr(M_s\,i[\rho_s,P_a])$, which is \eqref{eq:born-derivative}. Finally $Y\in[-1,1]$ (its spectrum lies in $[-\norm{M_s}_2,\norm{M_s}_2]$), so $\sigma_s^2(\theta)=\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid s)\leq\tfrac14(\,\mathrm{range}\,)^2\leq1$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Identifiability}
\label{app:inference-ident}

\begin{theorem}[Local identifiability of the residual coefficients]
\label{thm:identifiability}
Suppose the design satisfies the \emph{informative-design} condition: the Jacobian $J^\star:=J(\theta^\star)$ has rank $p$ (full column rank). Then the residual coefficient map $\theta\mapsto\theta$ is locally identifiable at $\theta^\star$---there is a neighborhood of $\theta^\star$ on which $\theta\mapsto g(\theta)$ is injective---and the Fisher information $\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)$ of the model is nonsingular. Moreover, the condition is met by an explicit Pauli design: for each basis operator $P_a$ take a setting $(\rho_a,M_a)$ with $\rho_a=\tfrac{1}{2^n}(\id+P_a)$ and $M_a=Q_a$ where $Q_a$ is any weight-$\leq w$ Pauli string with $\{Q_a,P_a\}=0$ and $\Tr(Q_aP_b\,i[\,\cdot\,])$ chosen so that the leading-order Jacobian $J^\star_0:=J(0)$ equals $2\,\mathrm{diag}$-dominant and invertible; in particular, with the canonical choice $Q_a=i[\rho_a,P_a]\cdot 2^n$ made a measurable observable, $J(0)$ is a nonzero scalar multiple of the identity, so $\operatorname{rank}J(0)=p$ and, for $\dt$ small enough, $\operatorname{rank}J(\theta^\star)=p$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Rank $\Rightarrow$ local identifiability.} By Lemma~\ref{lem:born-smooth}, $g$ is real-analytic, so $C^1$; if $J^\star=Dg(\theta^\star)$ has rank $p$ then $g$ is an immersion at $\theta^\star$, and the inverse-function theorem (applied to $g$ composed with a projection onto $p$ independent output coordinates) gives a neighborhood on which $g$ is injective. Hence distinct nearby $\theta$ produce distinct outcome-distribution means, so the parameter is locally identifiable.

\emph{Rank $\Rightarrow$ Fisher nonsingularity.} For a setting with mean $g_s(\theta)$, bounded outcome $Y\in[-1,1]$, and variance $\sigma_s^2(\theta)>0$, the per-shot score for the exponential/curved family has covariance dominated below by the delta-method form: the information contributed by $N_s$ i.i.d.\ shots at setting $s$ about $\theta$ is $N_s\,\sigma_s^{-2}(\theta)\,\nabla g_s\nabla g_s^\top$ in the Gaussian shot-noise limit (Theorem~\ref{thm:crb} makes this exact for the model there), so the total information is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{I}(\theta)=N\sum_{s}\pi_s\,\sigma_s^{-2}(\theta)\,\nabla g_s(\theta)\nabla g_s(\theta)^\top=N\,J(\theta)^\top\Xi\,\Sigma(\theta)^{-1}J(\theta).
    \label{eq:fisher-form}
\end{equation}
Because $\Xi\Sigma^{-1}\succ0$ (all $\pi_s>0$, all $\sigma_s^2\in(0,1]$), \eqref{eq:fisher-form} is a Gram matrix that is positive definite iff $J(\theta)$ has full column rank $p$; the hypothesis $\operatorname{rank}J^\star=p$ therefore gives $\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)\succ0$.

\emph{The explicit Pauli design.} Take the $p$ settings indexed by $a$ with $\rho_a=2^{-n}(\id+P_a)$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:born-smooth}, $J(0)_{a'a}=\Tr(M_{a'}\,i[\rho_a,P_a])$. Compute $i[\rho_a,P_a]=2^{-n}\,i[\id+P_a,P_a]=0$ pointwise---so the on-diagonal probe $\rho_a$ must be rotated. Replace $\rho_a$ by $\rho_a=2^{-n}(\id+P'_a)$ with $P'_a$ a weight-$\leq w$ Pauli string anticommuting with $P_a$ (one exists for every nonidentity Pauli string: flip a single tensor factor from $X\!\leftrightarrow\!Z$ on a site in $\supp P_a$, staying within weight $w$). Then $i[\rho_a,P_a]=2^{-n}i[P'_a,P_a]=2^{-n}\cdot2i\,P'_aP_a\cdot(\pm1)$ is $\pm2^{1-n}\,i\,(P'_aP_a)$, a nonzero Pauli string (up to phase) of weight $\leq w$. Choosing the readout $M_a$ to be that Pauli string makes $J(0)_{aa}=\Tr(M_a\,i[\rho_a,P_a])=\pm2^{1-n}\Tr\bigl((P'_aP_a)^2\bigr)\cdot(\text{phase})=\pm2$, a nonzero constant, while for $a'\neq a$ the readout $M_{a'}$ is a different Pauli string and $J(0)_{a'a}=2^{-n}\Tr(M_{a'}\,i[\,\cdot\,])$ is a Hilbert--Schmidt inner product of two \emph{distinct} Pauli strings, hence $0$ unless $M_{a'}\propto i[P'_a,P_a]$. Choosing the $p$ readouts to be distinct Pauli strings makes $J(0)=\pm2\,\Pi$ for a permutation matrix $\Pi$, so $\operatorname{rank}J(0)=p$. Finally $\theta\mapsto J(\theta)$ is continuous (Lemma~\ref{lem:born-smooth}) and $\theta^\star=O(\dt^{q+1})\to0$ as $\dt\to0$; since full rank is an open condition, there is $\dt_0>0$ with $\operatorname{rank}J(\theta^\star)=p$ for all $\dt\leq\dt_0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Identifiability is the statistical shadow of the Frobenius-optimality of Theorem~\ref{thm:pauli-projection}: the same orthogonality of weight-$\leq w$ Pauli strings that makes $\Pi_w$ a well-defined projection makes the leading-order design Jacobian a scaled permutation, hence full rank. Structure is what makes the residual an \emph{identifiable} estimand, not merely a definable one.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Cram\'er--Rao lower bound}
\label{app:inference-crb}

We now make the shot-noise model exact by passing to its local Gaussian form, which is the regime relevant to a finite-shot characterization of a small ($O(\dt^{q+1})$) residual and yields a clean, attainable bound. Concretely, let the setting-$s$ per-shot outcome be $Y_{s}=g_s(\theta)+\varepsilon_s$ with $\varepsilon_s$ zero-mean, variance $\sigma_s^2(\theta)$; the sufficient statistics are the sample means $\bar Y_s$, which by the central limit theorem are, for large $N_s$, $\bar Y_s\sim\mathcal{N}\!\bigl(g_s(\theta),\sigma_s^2(\theta)/N_s\bigr)$ independently across $s$. We take this Gaussian observation model as the estimation problem; Theorem~\ref{thm:mle} shows the exact multinomial MLE has the same first-order asymptotics.

\begin{theorem}[Cram\'er--Rao bound for the residual and its certificate]
\label{thm:crb}
Consider the Gaussian model $\bar Y_s\sim\mathcal{N}(g_s(\theta),\sigma_s^2(\theta^\star)/N_s)$, $s=1,\dots,S$, independent, with the informative-design condition $\operatorname{rank}J^\star=p$. Then the Fisher information is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)=N\,(J^\star)^\top\,\Xi\,\Sigma^{-1}\,J^\star\ \succ0,
    \qquad \Sigma=\Sigma(\theta^\star),
    \label{eq:crb-fisher}
\end{equation}
and every unbiased estimator $\hat\theta$ of the residual coefficients satisfies the matrix Cram\'er--Rao bound
\begin{equation}
    \operatorname{Cov}_{\theta^\star}(\hat\theta)\ \succeq\ \mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)^{-1}
    =\frac1N\bigl[(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star\bigr]^{-1}.
    \label{eq:crb-matrix}
\end{equation}
Consequently, writing the truncated residual operator estimate as $\widehat{\Pi_wK_q}=\sum_a\hat\theta_aP_a$, its Frobenius estimation error has variance lower-bounded by
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\,\norm{\widehat{\Pi_wK_q}-\Pi_wK_q}_F^2\ \geq\ 2^n\,\operatorname{tr}\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)^{-1},
    \label{eq:crb-frob}
\end{equation}
and the standard deviation of the resulting global certificate $r\dt$-scaled error is at least $r\,\sqrt{2^n\,\operatorname{tr}\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)^{-1}}\big/\text{(spectral-to-Frobenius factor)}$. The bound is attained in the limit $N\to\infty$ by the generalized-least-squares estimator of Theorem~\ref{thm:mle}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Fisher information.} For a single Gaussian observation $\bar Y_s\sim\mathcal{N}(g_s(\theta),v_s)$ with known variance $v_s=\sigma_s^2(\theta^\star)/N_s$, the log-likelihood is $\ell_s(\theta)=-\tfrac1{2v_s}(\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta))^2+\text{const}$, with score $\partial_{\theta_a}\ell_s=v_s^{-1}(\bar Y_s-g_s)\partial_{\theta_a}g_s$. The per-observation information is $[\mathcal{I}_s]_{ab}=\mathbb{E}[\partial_a\ell_s\,\partial_b\ell_s]=v_s^{-1}\partial_ag_s\partial_bg_s$, since $\mathbb{E}(\bar Y_s-g_s)^2=v_s$. Summing over independent settings and using $v_s^{-1}=N_s/\sigma_s^2=N\pi_s/\sigma_s^2$,
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)=\sum_s\frac{N\pi_s}{\sigma_s^2}\,\nabla g_s\nabla g_s^\top=N\,(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star,
\end{equation*}
which is \eqref{eq:crb-fisher}. Positive definiteness follows exactly as in the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:identifiability}: $\Xi\Sigma^{-1}\succ0$ and $\operatorname{rank}J^\star=p$ make the Gram matrix nonsingular.

\emph{Matrix CRB.} The model is a regular exponential/curved family: the log-likelihood is $C^2$, the score has mean zero ($\mathbb{E}\,\partial_a\ell=v_s^{-1}\mathbb{E}(\bar Y_s-g_s)\partial_ag_s=0$), and differentiation under the integral is justified because the Gaussian density and its $\theta$-derivatives are dominated by integrable functions uniformly on a neighborhood of $\theta^\star$. Under these standard regularity conditions the multivariate Cram\'er--Rao inequality holds: for any unbiased $\hat\theta$, $\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\theta)\succeq\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)^{-1}$. Explicitly, let $U=\hat\theta-\theta^\star$ and $V=\nabla_\theta\ell(\theta^\star)$ (the total score); unbiasedness gives $\mathbb{E}[U\,V^\top]=\partial_\theta\mathbb{E}_\theta[\hat\theta]\big|_{\theta^\star}=I_p$ (the $p\times p$ identity), while $\operatorname{Cov}(U)=\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\theta)$ and $\operatorname{Cov}(V)=\mathcal{I}$. The joint covariance $\begin{psmallmatrix}\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\theta)&I_p\\ I_p&\mathcal{I}\end{psmallmatrix}\succeq0$ is a valid covariance matrix, so its Schur complement is PSD: $\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\theta)-\mathcal{I}^{-1}\succeq0$. Substituting \eqref{eq:crb-fisher} yields \eqref{eq:crb-matrix}.

\emph{Frobenius consequence.} Since $\Tr(P_aP_b)=2^n\delta_{ab}$, the operators $\{2^{-n/2}P_a\}$ are Hilbert--Schmidt orthonormal, so $\norm{\sum_a(\hat\theta_a-\theta_a^\star)P_a}_F^2=2^n\sum_a(\hat\theta_a-\theta_a^\star)^2=2^n\norm{\hat\theta-\theta^\star}^2$. Taking expectations, $\mathbb{E}\norm{\widehat{\Pi_wK_q}-\Pi_wK_q}_F^2=2^n\operatorname{tr}\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\theta)\geq2^n\operatorname{tr}\mathcal{I}^{-1}$ by \eqref{eq:crb-matrix} and monotonicity of the trace under $\succeq$. The certificate statement follows by propagating this Frobenius bound through $\norm{\cdot}_2\leq\norm{\cdot}_F$ and the linear-in-$r$ certificate of Corollary~\ref{cor:projected-certificate}. Attainment is Theorem~\ref{thm:mle}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Equation~\eqref{eq:crb-matrix} is the precise sense in which the residual is ``certified rather than estimated'' only up to the information the design carries: as $N\to\infty$ the CRB vanishes and one recovers the deterministic certificate $\tau_w$ of Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}; at finite $N$ no unbiased estimator beats \eqref{eq:crb-matrix}. Because $\theta^\star=O(\dt^{q+1})$ the signal is small, so the design conditioning $\lambda_{\min}(\mathcal{I}/N)$---equivalently the $\kappa_w$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:certified-truncation}---controls the shot budget; this is the statistical price of the one place (Sec.~\ref{sec:learned}) where structure runs out and estimation is unavoidable.
\end{remark}

\subsection{MLE consistency, asymptotic normality, and efficiency}
\label{app:inference-mle}

\begin{theorem}[Consistency and asymptotic normality of the residual MLE]
\label{thm:mle}
Adopt the model of Theorem~\ref{thm:crb} with a compact parameter set $\Theta\ni\theta^\star$ in its interior, informative design $\operatorname{rank}J^\star=p$, and the identifiability of Theorem~\ref{thm:identifiability} (so $\theta\mapsto g(\theta)$ is injective on $\Theta$). Let
\begin{equation}
    \hat\theta_N=\arg\min_{\theta\in\Theta}\ \sum_{s=1}^{S}\frac{N_s}{\sigma_s^2(\theta^\star)}\bigl(\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta)\bigr)^2
    \label{eq:gls}
\end{equation}
be the (generalized-least-squares $=$ Gaussian maximum-likelihood) estimator. Then, as $N\to\infty$ with fixed allocations $\pi_s$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] $\hat\theta_N\xrightarrow{\ p\ }\theta^\star$ (consistency);
\item[(ii)] $\sqrt N\,(\hat\theta_N-\theta^\star)\xrightarrow{\ d\ }\mathcal{N}\!\bigl(0,\,\bigl[(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star\bigr]^{-1}\bigr)$ (asymptotic normality), i.e.\ $\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\theta_N)\to\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)^{-1}$;
\item[(iii)] $\hat\theta_N$ is asymptotically efficient: it attains the Cram\'er--Rao bound \eqref{eq:crb-matrix} in the limit.
\end{enumerate}
The same conclusions hold for the exact multinomial MLE from the finite outcome alphabet of each setting, with the same asymptotic covariance.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write $M_N(\theta)=\tfrac1N\sum_s\tfrac{N_s}{\sigma_s^2}(\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta))^2=\sum_s\tfrac{\pi_s}{\sigma_s^2}(\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta))^2$ and its population version $M(\theta)=\sum_s\tfrac{\pi_s}{\sigma_s^2}\bigl[(g_s(\theta^\star)-g_s(\theta))^2+\sigma_s^2/N_s\cdot 0\bigr]+\text{const}$; more precisely $\mathbb{E}[M_N(\theta)]=\sum_s\tfrac{\pi_s}{\sigma_s^2}\bigl((g_s(\theta^\star)-g_s(\theta))^2+v_s\bigr)$ with $v_s=\sigma_s^2/N_s\to0$. Define $M(\theta)=\sum_s\tfrac{\pi_s}{\sigma_s^2}(g_s(\theta^\star)-g_s(\theta))^2$.

\emph{(i) Consistency (Van der Vaart, M-estimation).} Three conditions give consistency of an M-estimator (minimizer of $M_N$) toward the minimizer of $M$: (a) uniform convergence $\sup_{\theta\in\Theta}\abs{M_N(\theta)-M(\theta)}\xrightarrow{p}0$; (b) a well-separated unique minimizer of $M$; (c) $M_N(\hat\theta_N)\leq M_N(\theta^\star)+o_p(1)$. For (a): $\bar Y_s\xrightarrow{p}g_s(\theta^\star)$ by the weak law, each $g_s$ is continuous on the compact $\Theta$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:born-smooth}), and $\Theta$ compact makes the finite sum of continuous functions converge uniformly (a finite sum of terms each of the form $\tfrac{\pi_s}{\sigma_s^2}(\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta))^2$ with $\bar Y_s\xrightarrow{p}$const and $g_s$ continuous is uniformly close to its limit). For (b): $M(\theta)\geq0$ with equality iff $g_s(\theta)=g_s(\theta^\star)$ for all $s$; by the injectivity of $\theta\mapsto g(\theta)$ on $\Theta$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:identifiability}, extended to global injectivity by the design hypothesis), this forces $\theta=\theta^\star$, so $\theta^\star$ is the unique minimizer, and continuity plus compactness make it well separated. (c) holds with equality since $\hat\theta_N$ minimizes $M_N$. Hence $\hat\theta_N\xrightarrow{p}\theta^\star$.

\emph{(ii) Asymptotic normality (delta method / Z-estimation).} $\hat\theta_N$ solves the stationarity equations $\Psi_N(\hat\theta_N)=0$ where $\Psi_N(\theta)=\nabla_\theta M_N(\theta)=-2\sum_s\tfrac{\pi_s}{\sigma_s^2}(\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta))\nabla g_s(\theta)$. A first-order Taylor expansion of $\Psi_N$ about $\theta^\star$ gives, at the (consistent) root,
\begin{equation*}
    0=\Psi_N(\theta^\star)+\bigl[\nabla\Psi_N(\tilde\theta_N)\bigr](\hat\theta_N-\theta^\star)
\end{equation*}
for some $\tilde\theta_N$ on the segment $[\hat\theta_N,\theta^\star]$, hence $\tilde\theta_N\xrightarrow{p}\theta^\star$. Now (using $\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta^\star)$ zero-mean with variance $v_s=\sigma_s^2/N_s$):
\begin{widetext}
\begin{align*}
    \sqrt N\,\Psi_N(\theta^\star)
    =-2\sum_s\frac{\pi_s}{\sigma_s^2}\,\sqrt N(\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta^\star))\,\nabla g_s(\theta^\star)
    \ \xrightarrow{\ d\ }\ \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(0,\,4\,\textstyle\sum_s\tfrac{\pi_s^2}{\sigma_s^4}\,\sigma_s^2\tfrac{N}{N_s}\,\nabla g_s\nabla g_s^\top\bigr)
    =\mathcal{N}\!\bigl(0,\,4\,(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star\bigr),
\end{align*}
\end{widetext}
because $\sqrt N(\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta^\star))\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_s^2/\pi_s)$ by the CLT (variance $N v_s=N\sigma_s^2/N_s=\sigma_s^2/\pi_s$) and the settings are independent; the resulting covariance is $4\sum_s\tfrac{\pi_s^2}{\sigma_s^4}\cdot\tfrac{\sigma_s^2}{\pi_s}\nabla g_s\nabla g_s^\top=4\sum_s\tfrac{\pi_s}{\sigma_s^2}\nabla g_s\nabla g_s^\top=4\,(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star$. For the Hessian, $\nabla\Psi_N(\tilde\theta_N)\xrightarrow{p}\nabla\Psi(\theta^\star)=2\sum_s\tfrac{\pi_s}{\sigma_s^2}\nabla g_s\nabla g_s^\top=2\,(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star=:2\,\mathsf{A}$ (the mean-square terms $(\bar Y_s-g_s)\nabla^2g_s$ vanish in probability because $\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta^\star)\xrightarrow{p}0$). Solving,
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation*}
    \sqrt N(\hat\theta_N-\theta^\star)=-\bigl[\nabla\Psi_N(\tilde\theta_N)\bigr]^{-1}\sqrt N\,\Psi_N(\theta^\star)\xrightarrow{\ d\ }(2\mathsf{A})^{-1}\mathcal{N}(0,4\mathsf{A})=\mathcal{N}\!\bigl(0,\mathsf{A}^{-1}\bigr),
\end{equation*}
\end{widetext}
by Slutsky's theorem, where $\mathsf{A}=(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star$. Thus $\operatorname{Cov}(\hat\theta_N)\to\tfrac1N\mathsf{A}^{-1}=\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)^{-1}$, proving (ii).

\emph{(iii) Efficiency.} The limiting covariance $\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)^{-1}$ equals the Cram\'er--Rao bound \eqref{eq:crb-matrix}, so $\hat\theta_N$ is asymptotically efficient.

\emph{Multinomial MLE.} For the exact model, each setting $s$ has finitely many outcomes with probabilities $\{p_{s,o}(\theta)\}$ that are real-analytic in $\theta$ (polynomials in the entries of $R_q(\theta)$), and $g_s(\theta)=\sum_o m_{s,o}p_{s,o}(\theta)$. The exact MLE maximizes $\sum_s\sum_o N_{s,o}\log p_{s,o}(\theta)$. Under the same compactness, identifiability, and interior-point conditions, classical MLE theory (Van der Vaart, Thm.~5.39) gives consistency and $\sqrt N(\hat\theta^{\mathrm{ML}}_N-\theta^\star)\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{full}}(\theta^\star)^{-1})$, where $\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{full}}$ is the full outcome-multinomial information. Since the outcome mean $g_s$ is the only functional entering the certificate and the Gaussian model uses exactly the information of $g_s$, the two informations agree at leading order in the small-residual regime, and the certificate covariance is $\mathcal{I}(\theta^\star)^{-1}$ as stated.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Certified spectral truncation from finite samples}
\label{app:inference-certified}

The final result gives the honest finite-sample guarantee: the estimated \emph{and} truncated residual is close to the \emph{true} (untruncated) residual, splitting deterministic truncation bias from statistical error.

\begin{theorem}[Finite-sample certified truncation]
\label{thm:certified-truncation}
Fix $q$, $\dt$, level $w$, an informative design with $\operatorname{rank}J^\star=p$, and let $\hat\theta_N$ be the estimator of Theorem~\ref{thm:mle} with plug-in operator $\widehat\Pi_wK_q=\sum_{a=1}^{p}\hat\theta_aP_a$. Define the design-conditioning constant
\begin{equation}
    \kappa_w:=\frac{\max_s\sigma_s^{-1}}{\sqrt{\lambda_{\min}\!\bigl((J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star\bigr)}},
    \label{eq:kappa}
\end{equation}
a finite constant under the rank condition. Assume the per-shot outcomes are bounded in $[-1,1]$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:born-smooth}) and, for the concentration step, that $\hat\theta$ is the linearized (one-Newton-step) GLS estimator, so that $\hat\theta_a-\theta_a^\star$ is a bounded-coefficient linear combination of the centered shot averages. Then for every $\delta\in(0,1)$, with probability at least $1-\delta$,
\begin{equation}
    \norm{K_q-\widehat\Pi_wK_q}_2\ \le\ \tau_w\ +\ \kappa_w\,p\sqrt{\frac{2}{N}\,\log\frac{2p}{\delta}},
    \label{eq:certified-truncation}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_w=\norm{K_q-\Pi_wK_q}_2$ is the deterministic truncation tail of Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}; when the retained templates act on pairwise-disjoint or mutually-commuting supports (the geometrically local set of Sec.~\ref{sec:learned}, Theorem~\ref{thm:tfim-support}) the operator-norm-to-coefficient bound sharpens and the prefactor $p$ improves to $\sqrt p$, giving the $\kappa_w\sqrt{2p/N\,\log(2p/\delta)}$ form of \eqref{eq:certified-truncation-intro}. Consequently the level-$w$ estimated corrected step $\widehat G_{q,w}=e^{-i\widehat\Pi_wK_q}S_q$ meets a global $(\epsilon,\delta)$ guarantee,
\begin{equation}
    \Pr\Bigl[\ \norm{\widehat G_{q,w}^{\,r}-U(t)}_2\le\epsilon\ \Bigr]\ \ge\ 1-\delta,
    \label{eq:certified-global}
\end{equation}
whenever the level $w\geq w^\star(\epsilon/2)$ of Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth} (so $r\tau_w\leq\epsilon/2$) and the shot budget satisfies
\begin{equation}
    N\ \geq\ \frac{8\,p^2\,r^2\,\kappa_w^2}{\epsilon^2}\,\log\frac{2p}{\delta}
    \qquad\Bigl(\tfrac{8\,p\,r^2\,\kappa_w^2}{\epsilon^2}\log\tfrac{2p}{\delta}\ \text{under local support}\Bigr).
    \label{eq:shot-budget}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Bias--variance split.} By the triangle inequality and $\Pi_w$ being an orthogonal projection,
\begin{equation}
    \norm{K_q-\widehat\Pi_wK_q}_2\leq\underbrace{\norm{K_q-\Pi_wK_q}_2}_{=\tau_w}+\underbrace{\norm{\Pi_wK_q-\widehat\Pi_wK_q}_2}_{\text{statistical}}.
    \label{eq:split}
\end{equation}
The first term is deterministic and equals $\tau_w$; by Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate} it is geometrically small, $\tau_w\leq C_q\rho^w$. The second is the operator norm of $\sum_a(\theta_a^\star-\hat\theta_a)P_a$; since each Pauli string has $\norm{P_a}_2=1$, the triangle inequality gives the coefficient bound
\begin{equation}
    \norm{\Pi_wK_q-\widehat\Pi_wK_q}_2\leq\sum_{a=1}^{p}\abs{\hat\theta_a-\theta_a^\star}\cdot\norm{P_a}_2=\norm{\hat\theta-\theta^\star}_1 .
    \label{eq:op-to-coeff}
\end{equation}

\emph{Concentration of the coefficient error.} The linearized GLS estimator is
\begin{equation*}
    \hat\theta-\theta^\star=\mathsf{A}^{-1}(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}\,\xi,
    \qquad
    \mathsf{A}=(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star,
\end{equation*}
where $\xi_s=\bar Y_s-g_s(\theta^\star)$ is the centered shot average at setting $s$, a mean-zero average of $N_s$ i.i.d.\ bounded variables $Y\in[-1,1]$. Hence each coordinate is $\hat\theta_a-\theta_a^\star=\sum_s b_{as}\xi_s$ with row coefficients $b_{as}=[\mathsf{A}^{-1}(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}]_{as}$. Expanding $\xi_s=\tfrac1{N_s}\sum_{k=1}^{N_s}(Y_{s,k}-g_s(\theta^\star))$ writes $\hat\theta_a-\theta_a^\star$ as a sum of $N$ independent, mean-zero terms, each of magnitude $\leq2\abs{b_{as}}/N_s$. By Hoeffding's inequality, for each $a$,
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation*}
    \Pr\bigl[\abs{\hat\theta_a-\theta_a^\star}\geq u\bigr]\leq2\exp\!\Bigl(-\frac{u^2}{2\sum_s N_s(2\abs{b_{as}}/N_s)^2/4}\Bigr)=2\exp\!\Bigl(-\frac{u^2}{2\sum_s b_{as}^2/N_s}\Bigr).
\end{equation*}
\end{widetext}
It remains to bound the per-coordinate variance proxy $v_a:=\sum_s b_{as}^2/N_s$. With $B=\mathsf{A}^{-1}(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}$ and $N_s=N\pi_s$,
\begin{equation*}
    v_a=\tfrac1N\sum_s\frac{b_{as}^2}{\pi_s}=\tfrac1N\bigl[B\,\Xi^{-1}B^\top\bigr]_{aa}\leq\tfrac1N\,\lambda_{\max}\!\bigl(B\,\Xi^{-1}B^\top\bigr).
\end{equation*}
Since $\Xi,\Sigma$ are diagonal and positive (hence mutually commuting), a direct computation gives $B\Xi^{-1}B^\top=\mathsf{A}^{-1}(J^\star)^\top\,\Xi\Sigma^{-2}\,J^\star\mathsf{A}^{-1}$. Because $\Sigma^{-2}=\Sigma^{-1}\Sigma^{-1}\preceq(\max_s\sigma_s^{-2})\,\Sigma^{-1}$ as diagonal matrices, we have $\Xi\Sigma^{-2}\preceq(\max_s\sigma_s^{-2})\,\Xi\Sigma^{-1}$, so
\begin{equation*}
    B\Xi^{-1}B^\top\ \preceq\ (\max_s\sigma_s^{-2})\,\mathsf{A}^{-1}(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star\mathsf{A}^{-1}=(\max_s\sigma_s^{-2})\,\mathsf{A}^{-1},
\end{equation*}
using $(J^\star)^\top\Xi\Sigma^{-1}J^\star=\mathsf{A}$. Hence $\lambda_{\max}(B\Xi^{-1}B^\top)\leq(\max_s\sigma_s^{-2})/\lambda_{\min}(\mathsf{A})=\kappa_w^2$ by the definition \eqref{eq:kappa} of $\kappa_w$. Therefore $v_a\leq\kappa_w^2/N$ for every $a$. Setting
\begin{equation*}
    u=\kappa_w\sqrt{\tfrac2N\log\tfrac{2p}{\delta}}
\end{equation*}
makes each coordinate's failure probability at most $\delta/p$; a union bound over $a=1,\dots,p$ gives, with probability $\geq1-\delta$, $\abs{\hat\theta_a-\theta_a^\star}\leq u$ simultaneously for all $a$. On this event $\norm{\hat\theta-\theta^\star}_1\leq p\,u$, so by \eqref{eq:op-to-coeff},
\begin{equation*}
    \norm{\Pi_wK_q-\widehat\Pi_wK_q}_2\leq p\,u=\kappa_w\,p\sqrt{\tfrac2N\log\tfrac{2p}\delta}.
\end{equation*}
Combining with \eqref{eq:split} gives the certificate
\begin{equation*}
    \norm{K_q-\widehat\Pi_wK_q}_2\leq\tau_w+\kappa_w\,p\sqrt{\tfrac2N\log\tfrac{2p}\delta}
    \qquad\text{w.p.}\ \geq1-\delta,
\end{equation*}
which is \eqref{eq:certified-truncation}. When the retained $P_a$ have pairwise-disjoint or mutually-commuting supports (the geometrically local template set of Sec.~\ref{sec:learned}, Theorem~\ref{thm:tfim-support}), the operator norm coincides with the coefficient $\ell_2$ norm, $\norm{\sum_a(\hat\theta_a-\theta^\star_a)P_a}_2=\norm{\hat\theta-\theta^\star}_2\leq\sqrt p\,u$, which sharpens the prefactor $p$ to $\sqrt p$ and reproduces exactly the $\sqrt{2p/N}$ form quoted in \eqref{eq:certified-truncation-intro}. We carry the general prefactor $p$ below; the local-support case simply replaces $p^2$ by $p$ throughout.

\emph{Global guarantee.} By the Duhamel bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:duhamel}), $\norm{e^{-i\widehat\Pi_wK_q}-e^{-iK_q}}_2\leq\norm{K_q-\widehat\Pi_wK_q}_2$, and both are unitary, so the sharp multi-step bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:residual-stability}) gives $\norm{\widehat G_{q,w}^{\,r}-U(t)}_2\leq r\,\norm{K_q-\widehat\Pi_wK_q}_2$. On the probability-$\geq1-\delta$ event of \eqref{eq:certified-truncation}, and using $w\geq w^\star(\epsilon/2)$ so that $r\tau_w\leq\epsilon/2$,
\begin{equation*}
    \norm{\widehat G_{q,w}^{\,r}-U(t)}_2\leq r\tau_w+r\,\kappa_w\,p\sqrt{\tfrac{2}N\log\tfrac{2p}\delta}\leq\frac\epsilon2+r\,\kappa_w\,p\sqrt{\tfrac{2}N\log\tfrac{2p}\delta}.
\end{equation*}
The second term is $\leq\epsilon/2$ exactly when $r^2\kappa_w^2p^2\tfrac{2}N\log\tfrac{2p}\delta\leq\epsilon^2/4$, i.e.\ $N\geq8p^2r^2\kappa_w^2\epsilon^{-2}\log(2p/\delta)$, which is \eqref{eq:shot-budget}. Then the total is $\leq\epsilon$ with probability $\geq1-\delta$, which is \eqref{eq:certified-global}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Shot complexity of a certified learned correction]
\label{cor:shot-complexity}
Under the geometric regime of Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate} ($\tau_w\leq C_q\rho^w$, $\rho=O(\dt)$), a global $(\epsilon,\delta)$ certificate for the estimated truncated correction is achieved with truncation level
\begin{equation*}
    w^\star=\Bigl\lceil\log\!\tfrac{2rC_q}{\epsilon}\big/\log\tfrac1\rho\Bigr\rceil
    \quad\text{and}\quad
    N=O\!\Bigl(\frac{p\,r^2\,\kappa_w^2}{\epsilon^2}\log\frac p\delta\Bigr)\ \text{shots.}
\end{equation*}
The truncation level---the deterministic half of the certificate---grows only logarithmically in $1/\epsilon$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}), while the shot count---the statistical half---grows as $\epsilon^{-2}$, the canonical estimation rate. Thus structure removes the $\epsilon^{-2}$ cost from all but the finite, size-independent template coefficients that must be learned, which is the quantitative content of ``certified where possible, estimated only where necessary.''
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The level follows from Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth} applied with target $\epsilon/2$ and the geometric tail $\tau_w\leq C_q\rho^w$: $r\tau_{w}\leq\epsilon/2$ as soon as $w\geq\log(2rC_q/\epsilon)/\log(1/\rho)$, and $w^\star$ is the least such integer. The shot count is \eqref{eq:shot-budget}, with $\log(2p/\delta)=O(\log(p/\delta))$. The dependence on $p$ (the number of retained templates) is size-independent because, by Theorems~\ref{thm:tfim-support} and~\ref{thm:order-locality}, the weight-$\leq w$ local template count per site is bounded independently of $n$, and $\kappa_w$ is a property of the fixed local design.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:certified-truncation} is the inference-theoretic completion of the paper's thesis. The two terms of \eqref{eq:certified-truncation} are exactly the two modes of the framework: $\tau_w$ is the \emph{deterministic} truncation certificate (Corollary~\ref{cor:bandwidth}, Fig.~\ref{fig:spectral}c), free of any confidence level and governed by the geometric rate of Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-rate}; the second term is the \emph{statistical} error of the one place where estimation cannot be avoided (Sec.~\ref{sec:learned}), governed by the Cram\'er--Rao geometry of Theorem~\ref{thm:crb} and the efficiency of Theorem~\ref{thm:mle}. Because the manuscript's reported experiments compute or certify the residual rather than sample it, this appendix adds an inference layer---an a priori bound on the estimation error \emph{were} the coefficients measured on hardware---without changing any reported number, and it makes precise the sample cost that the certified truncation is designed to avoid.
\end{remark}

\section{Detailed BCH support analysis for the TFIM}
\label{app:bch}

The main-text support theorem deliberately avoids exact BCH coefficients. Here we make the underlying structure explicit. For Strang splitting, $S_2(\dt)=e^{-iB\dt/2}e^{-iA\dt}e^{-iB\dt/2}$, the logarithm has the form $\log S_2(\dt)=-i(A+B)\dt+\dt^3\Gamma_3+O(\dt^5)$, where $\Gamma_3$ is a linear combination of $[A,[A,B]]$ and $[B,[A,B]]$. The absence of an even power follows from time-reversal symmetry of the Strang formula. The residual is $R_2(\dt)=e^{-i(A+B)\dt}S_2(\dt)^\dagger$, and the leading residual generator cancels the leading logarithmic defect, so $K_2(\dt)=\dt^3K_2^{(3)}+O(\dt^5)$, where $K_2^{(3)}$ is a real linear combination of $i[A,[A,B]]$ and $i[B,[A,B]]$ after accounting for the anti-Hermitian nature of commutators of Hermitian matrices.

For the \tfim{}, write $A_e=JZ_jZ_{j+1}$ and $B_v=hX_v$. The elementary commutator is $[A_e,B_v]=0$ if $v\notin e$. If $v=j$, then $[Z_jZ_{j+1},X_j]=2iY_jZ_{j+1}$, and if $v=j+1$, then $[Z_jZ_{j+1},X_{j+1}]=2iZ_jY_{j+1}$. Thus $[A,B]$ is a sum of weight-two Pauli strings on nearest-neighbor edges. Next consider $[B,[A,B]]$: a term $[B_{v'},[A_e,B_v]]$ is nonzero only if $v'$ lies in the support of $[A_e,B_v]$, which is the same edge $e$; the resulting Pauli string remains supported on that edge and has weight at most two, for example $[X_j,Y_jZ_{j+1}]=2iZ_jZ_{j+1}$ and $[X_{j+1},Y_jZ_{j+1}]=-2iY_jY_{j+1}$. Now consider $[A,[A,B]]$: a term $[A_{e'},[A_e,B_v]]$ is nonzero only if $e'$ overlaps the support of $[A_e,B_v]$. In a one-dimensional nearest-neighbor chain, the union of two overlapping edges contains at most three sites, $(j-1,j,j+1)$ or $(j,j+1,j+2)$. Therefore every nonzero Pauli string in $[A,[A,B]]$ has weight at most three, proving that the leading Strang residual generator is contained in $\cB_3$. The same proof strategy extends to other local Hamiltonians: if the interaction hypergraph has local terms of diameter at most $r_0$, then an $m$-fold nested commutator can only grow along overlapping local supports, connecting residual-generator compression to locality bounds and Lieb--Robinson ideas~\cite{lieb1972,tran2020}.

\section{Projection formulas and diagnostic quantities}
\label{app:projection}

For $n$ qubits, the Pauli group modulo phases gives $4^n$ Hermitian strings satisfying $\Tr(PQ)=2^n\delta_{P,Q}$. Every Hermitian matrix $K$ has an expansion $K=\sum_{P\in\cP_n} c_P P$ with $c_P=\Tr(PK)/2^n$, squared Frobenius norm $\norm{K}_F^2=2^n\sum_{P\in\cP_n}\abs{c_P}^2$, and cumulative coefficient mass (plotted in Fig.~\ref{edfig:genstruct}a) $M(w)=\sum_{\operatorname{wt}(P)\leq w}\abs{c_P}^2/\sum_{P\in\cP_n}\abs{c_P}^2$. For the $n=5$, $q=2$, $\dt=0.1$ run, the generated squared coefficient masses by weight are $M_0'=3.598\times10^{-33}$, $M_1'=1.466\times10^{-6}$, $M_2'=9.973\times10^{-7}$, $M_3'=1.262\times10^{-6}$, $M_4'=1.235\times10^{-10}$, and $M_5'=3.413\times10^{-16}$. Although the weight-four mass is small in Frobenius terms, it can still matter for spectral-norm accuracy after repeated steps, which is why Table~\ref{tab:projected-summary} shows a large improvement from $w=3$ to $w=4$.

\section{Finite-precision interpretation}
\label{app:finite-precision}

The exact residual theorem is an algebraic identity. In floating-point arithmetic, the computed residual is $\widetilde R_q=\operatorname{fl}\{\widetilde U(\dt)\widetilde S_q(\dt)^\dagger\}$ and the computed corrected step is $\widetilde G_q=\operatorname{fl}\{\widetilde R_q\widetilde S_q\}$. Round-off enters through every matrix exponential and multiplication. This explains why the $q=8$ baseline, already extremely accurate at $\dt=0.1$, can appear slightly more accurate than the oracle residual in Table~\ref{tab:error-summary}: the theorem says the exact-arithmetic error is zero, while the table reports floating-point evaluations of different arithmetic circuits. For low and moderate order, the product-formula error dominates round-off and oracle \rgtc{} gives many orders of magnitude improvement; for very high order at small step size, product-formula error can be below or comparable to double-precision round-off, so differences at $10^{-13}$ are not physically meaningful; projected residual experiments should be judged at error levels well above round-off, such as the $w=3$ and $w=4$ results in Table~\ref{tab:projected-summary}.

\section{Reproducibility checklist}
\label{app:repro}

A reproducible implementation should verify $\norm{S_q^\dagger S_q-\id}_2<10^{-12}$ for every tested $q$, $n$, and $\dt$; verify $\norm{R_q^\dagger R_q-\id}_2<10^{-12}$ for every oracle residual; verify $\norm{R_qS_q-U(\dt)}_2<10^{-12}$ for small and moderate orders; save raw CSV or JSON data before plotting; make plots only from saved raw data, not from hard-coded arrays; record package versions and BLAS backend information; use deterministic parameter grids or fixed random seeds; and include tests that fail if a citation key in \texttt{main.tex} is missing from \texttt{refs.bib}. These checks are especially important if the code is ported to GPU backends, where matrix-exponential implementations and floating-point accumulation order may differ from CPU reference values.

\section{Possible extensions}
\label{app:extensions}

The residual-generator framework suggests several concrete research extensions. A \emph{BCH residual compiler} derives explicit nested-commutator formulas for $K_q$ through a target order and implements $e^{-iK_q^{\mathrm{BCH}}}$ as a short product formula; Theorems~\ref{thm:residual-stability} and~\ref{thm:duhamel} convert truncation error into global simulation error. A \emph{variational residual compiler} uses the oracle residual on small systems as a target for a parameterized local circuit $V(\theta)$, with loss $\norm{V(\theta)-R_q}_F^2$, local Pauli-shadow loss, or state-averaged infidelity, and the stability theorem provides the operator-norm target. An \emph{operator-learning residual compiler} is realized in Sec.~\ref{sec:learned} for the Strang \tfim{}, learning the step-size-conditioned local weight-$\leq3$ coefficients of $K_2$ with a single size-transferable network trained from oracle-free local patches; natural continuations extend the local templates to other lattices and higher orders, replace patch labels with classical-shadow or commutator estimates on hardware, and compile the learned generator $e^{-i\widehat K_q}$ into native gates using Theorems~\ref{thm:residual-stability} and~\ref{thm:duhamel} as the operator-norm target. A \emph{GPU-accelerated benchmark generator} uses CPU dense simulation as a correctness reference, then ports parameter sweeps and Pauli projections to batched GPU kernels, keeping output tolerance-comparable to the CPU reference and always regenerating raw data before producing figures.
